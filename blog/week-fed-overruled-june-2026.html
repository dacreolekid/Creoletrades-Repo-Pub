The Week the Fed Got Overruled

How a peace deal and a chip melt-up steamrolled a hawkish Fed — and what it means for next week.

✍ Coach Que 📅 June 19, 2026 ⏱ ~6 min read 🏷 FOMC · Semis · Peace Deal

A hawkish Fed should have been the story. It wasn’t even the headline by Thursday.

The Fed played the villain

Kevin Warsh’s first FOMC, Wednesday afternoon, did the hawkish thing: the committee held at 3.75% but raised the dot plot at every horizon — 2026 to 3.8% from 3.4%, the easing bias gone, the lone projected cut erased. The 2026 dot now sits above the current rate, which is the market’s way of saying “no cuts, and don’t rule out a hike.” The tape took it straight: the S&P futures fell about a percent, the VIX jumped 12% to 18.43, the 2-year yield ripped 3.2%, the dollar firmed, and gold got hit. Textbook hawkish reaction — a bear-flattener with the front end leading.

If that’s all that happened, we’d be writing a very different recap.

Then Trump overruled the Fed

Hours after the dots dropped, the President signed the draft US–Iran peace deal at Versailles — ending the Iran War, reopening the Strait of Hormuz, with the formal ceremony set for Friday. For anyone who’s been reading the Journal since the spring — the Hormuz scare, the VIX above 29, oil over $110 — this was the other shoe finally dropping, the good way. Call it the TACO trade paying off: the de-escalation the market had been positioning for came early, and it came clean. Oil bled the war premium out all week, and the geopolitical “all-clear” gave traders permission to buy the hawkish dip.

Thursday was the rip

The reversal was violent. Nasdaq futures +2.3%, QQQ +2.3%, small caps +1.6%, and the VIX crushed 9% back to ~16.5. Yields fell and bonds caught a bid as the hawkish repricing partly unwound. The tell the whole time: credit never cracked — high-yield spreads stayed calm right through Wednesday’s flush, which is the single reason to read that selloff as contained, not the start of something. The fear came in on Wednesday and left on Thursday.

The leader: Intel and the chip trade

The cleanest trade of the week wasn’t an index — it was Intel. The President spent the week flexing the government’s 10% stake in INTC (now worth north of $60 billion as the stock ran its valuation from ~$100B to ~$600B, making Uncle Sam its largest shareholder, ahead of Vanguard and BlackRock) and touting Intel’s US-chipmaking partnership with Apple. INTC went vertical — up double digits Thursday into fresh all-time highs — and dragged the whole semiconductor complex with it (the 3x semis ETF closed up roughly nineteen percent on the day). Chip nationalism plus risk-on is a powerful cocktail, and the AI-infrastructure stack — chips, the data centers that house them, the power grid that feeds them — all caught the bid.

Mind the calendar

None of this happened in a vacuum. Quad-witching expiration landed Thursday, and Friday was closed for Juneteenth — so a vertical, news-driven move got jammed into a three-day-weekend gap. Big expiration plus thin holiday liquidity plus a parabolic tape is exactly the recipe for traps when the market reopens.

The takeaway

The lesson of the week is one we come back to a lot: catalysts overrule structure in the short run. The Fed turned hawkish and it simply didn’t matter, because a peace dividend and a policy-driven chip melt-up were bigger forces on the tape. The risk now is that the rip is event-driven and extended — Intel and the semis are parabolic, and parabolic moves into resting highs get run, then snap. So the edge into next week isn’t chasing the vertical; it’s buying the pullback in the leaders and watching two things: whether the dollar finally rolls and gold turns up. Until those two confirm, this is a relief rip with a geopolitical tailwind — a strong one, but a relief rip — not yet a confirmed dovish risk-on.

This is the resolution of the arc we opened in the spring with Oil, Conflict, and the VIX: the war premium that drove crude over $110 finally came out, and it came out fast.

Read the chart, not the news. But this week, the news read the chart right back.
