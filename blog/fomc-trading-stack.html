How to Trade Around FOMC Without Predicting the Outcome

FOMC week doesn't require a prediction. It requires a process. Here's how STACK turns the Fed's most volatile days into the clearest setups of the month.

✍ Coach Que 📅 March 17, 2026 ⏱ ~7 min read 🏷 Trading Education

Every six to eight weeks, the Federal Open Market Committee meets to decide the direction of US interest rates. Every six to eight weeks, the majority of retail traders spend the 48 hours before that decision making predictions, taking positions based on those predictions, and then getting stopped out when the market reacts in a way they didn't expect.

There's a better way. And it starts with understanding what you're actually dealing with when FOMC week arrives.

What FOMC Actually Does to the Market

The Fed's rate decision doesn't just move the market because of what it decides — it moves the market because of the uncertainty leading up to it and the repricing that follows. In the days before a decision, implied volatility rises as options traders buy protection against a surprise move. After the decision, that volatility gets released — often violently — as the market reprices based on what was said, what was implied, and what the Fed's tone suggested about future decisions.

This is why attempting to predict FOMC direction is a low-probability exercise. You're not just predicting the decision itself — you're predicting how traders will interpret the language, how algos will react in the first 30 seconds, and whether the initial move will reverse. Three variables layered on top of each other, any one of which can invalidate a position built on the other two.

The market will tell you what the Fed decision confirmed. Your job is to be positioned to listen — not to guess before the mic turns on.

How STACK Applies Around a News Event

STACK doesn't pause for news events. If anything, news events are where STACK becomes most important — because the temptation to abandon structure and react to headlines is at its highest.

S — Structure Before the Announcement

Before FOMC day, your job is to map the structural field. Where are the key levels on the monthly, weekly, and daily charts? Where has price rejected before? Where are the unfilled gaps or volume voids that could act as magnets if price breaks in either direction? This work happens before the news drops — not during. You're building your map so you know exactly what a bullish confirmation looks like versus a bearish one. When the decision hits, you're not scrambling. You're watching to see which path price takes.

T — Trigger Only After Confirmed Reaction

This is the most violated rule during news events. Traders see the initial candle print, assume direction, and enter. Then the market reverses. The trigger rule in STACK is non-negotiable during high-impact news: you do not trigger until the initial reaction has settled and price has begun to show a directional commitment. That often means waiting 15–30 minutes after the announcement. You will miss the first move. That is the correct outcome. The first move after FOMC is frequently a trap. The second move — after the dust settles — is where the real trade lives.

A — Alignment Across Timeframes Post-Decision

Once the initial reaction has printed, check alignment. Is the daily now aligned with the weekly? Did the move confirm or reject a key structural level? Alignment post-FOMC gives you the context to understand whether the move has legs or whether it's a one-day event that will mean-revert. A decision that causes the daily to break a major level and align with the weekly trend is a meaningful signal. A decision that causes a 30-minute spike that immediately retraces is noise.

⚜ The STACK Rule for News Events Structure is built before the announcement. Trigger is defined after the reaction settles. Alignment is confirmed across timeframes. Confirmation is the final gate before any entry. Discipline means honoring all of the above even when the initial move looks tempting.

What the March 2026 FOMC Showed Us

The March 17–18 FOMC decision lands inside one of the most complex macro environments in recent memory: VIX above 29, oil elevated on Strait of Hormuz disruption, and inflation data that gives the Fed no clean path to cut. The market was not pricing in a cut going into the meeting — it was pricing in a hold and watching closely for language about the future path.

This is exactly the kind of FOMC setup where STACK pays off. The trader who mapped structure before the decision knew exactly which levels mattered. The trader who defined their trigger post-reaction avoided the initial volatility spike. The trader who waited for alignment across timeframes before committing understood whether the move had follow-through.
