Welcome to the STACK Academy Glossary. This comprehensive reference guide defines all key terms and concepts used throughout the course. Use this glossary to review definitions, deepen your understanding, and quickly look up terminology as you progress through the modules.

A-C

Alignment (A in STACK)
When all timeframes (Monthly, Weekly, Daily, 4-Hour, 2-Hour) agree on direction. Alignment = 1 point on the Roux Score. Perfect alignment across all five timeframes signals maximum institutional consensus and highest probability setups.
Bearish
Price is trending downward. Sellers are in control. In bearish structure, each swing low is lower than the previous one, and each swing high is lower than the previous high. Bearish markets offer short trade opportunities.
Break of Structure (BOS)
When price breaks past a recent swing high (in an uptrend) or swing low (in a downtrend), confirming the current trend is continuing. A BOS is a continuation signal—it tells you the trend still has momentum behind it.
Breaker Block
A failed order block. When an order block gets broken through, it can flip from support to resistance (or vice versa) and become a new trading zone. Smart Money often uses breaker blocks as levels to trade in the opposite direction.
Bullish
Price is trending upward. Buyers are in control. In bullish structure, each swing high is higher than the previous one, and each swing low is higher than the previous low. Bullish markets offer long trade opportunities.
Candle
A single bar on a price chart. Each candle shows four prices: open, close, high, and low. The thick part is the "body" (open-to-close range) and the thin lines are "wicks" (how far price reached beyond the body). The size and shape of candles reveal Smart Money's behavior.
Change of Character (CHoCH)
The first break in the opposite direction of the current trend. It signals a potential reversal but requires further confirmation before trading. A CHoCH is an early warning signal that the trend structure may be shifting.
Confirmation (C in STACK)
The step where Wolf Chart 2.1 confirms that momentum supports your trade thesis. Confirmation = 1 point on the Roux Score. Without confirmation, you have no entry signal, regardless of how perfect the setup looks.
Consolidation
A period where price moves sideways in a tight range, neither trending up nor down. Often happens before a big move as Smart Money accumulates or distributes positions. Consolidation phases are when institutional players gather quietly before making their move.

D-H

Demand Zone
A price area where buyers previously stepped in aggressively. Price is expected to bounce when it returns to this level. Think of it as a "floor" where buying pressure lives. Demand zones are areas where Smart Money accumulated positions.
Discount
The lower half of a price range. Smart Money prefers to buy in the discount zone (below the midpoint of a range). This is institutional risk management—buy low, accumulate on weakness.
Displacement
A strong, fast price move away from a key level. Large candle bodies with small wicks signal that Smart Money is behind the move. Displacement shows institutional intent and conviction in one direction without hesitation.
Engulfing Candle
A candle whose body completely covers (engulfs) the body of the previous candle. A bullish engulfing candle closes higher than the previous candle opened. A bearish engulfing candle closes lower. Engulfing candles often signal strong reversal moves.
External Structure
The larger, higher-timeframe structure that "contains" smaller price movements. The primary trend viewed from a zoomed-out perspective. In top-down analysis, external structure sets the bias for your entry on lower timeframes.
Fair Value Gap (FVG)
A three-candle pattern where the middle candle moves so fast that a gap is left between the first and third candle's wicks. Price tends to return and "fill" this gap before continuing. FVGs are imbalances that Smart Money created and will exploit.

I-L

Imbalance
Any area on the chart where price moved too quickly, leaving unfilled orders. Fair Value Gaps are one type of imbalance. Imbalances represent inefficiencies that the market will eventually correct.
Institutional Traders
Large market participants (banks, hedge funds, pension funds) that move markets with massive orders. Also called "Smart Money." Institutional traders control the liquidity and direction of markets, and STACK teaches you to read their footprints.
Internal Structure
The smaller, lower-timeframe structure that unfolds within the external (higher-timeframe) structure. The day-to-day movements within the big picture. Internal structure details are where you find your specific entry and exit triggers.
Invalidation
The price level where your trade thesis is proven wrong. This is where you place your stop loss. If price hits your invalidation level, you exit—no exceptions. Invalidation is non-negotiable in STACK trading.
Keep Discipline (K in STACK)
The final letter: correct position sizing, invalidation set, and trade plan written before entry. K = 1 point on the Roux Score. Discipline separates professional traders from amateurs.
Liquidity
Clusters of stop-loss orders or pending orders sitting at obvious price levels (above highs, below lows). Smart Money often pushes price into these areas to fill large orders. Understanding where liquidity pools exist is crucial to predicting Smart Money's next move.
Liquidity Sweep
When price briefly pushes past a key level (taking out stop losses) and then reverses. Smart Money uses sweeps to fill large orders before moving price in their intended direction. A liquidity sweep is a trap designed to shake out weak hands.

M-P

Mitigation
When price returns to an order block and "fills" the orders left there. A mitigated order block has already been visited; an unmitigated one has not been tested yet. Mitigation is confirmation that an order block was real and active.
Order Block (OB)
The last opposing candle before a strong impulsive move. Bullish OB = last red candle before a big move up. Bearish OB = last green candle before a big move down. These zones are where Smart Money placed their orders and are high-probability trading levels.
Premium
The upper half of a price range. Smart Money prefers to sell in the premium zone (above the midpoint of a range). Premium is where sellers are strongest and Smart Money distributes (sells) positions into buyer demand.
PT1 / PT2 / PT3
The three profit targets in a STACK trade. PT1: first target where you trim 30-50%. PT2: second target where you trim more. PT3: the full extension where remaining shares ride. Taking profits in stages locks in gains while maintaining upside exposure.

R-S

Retail Traders
Individual traders (like most of us starting out) who trade with smaller accounts. Retail traders often get caught on the wrong side when Smart Money moves price. Understanding retail behavior helps you avoid being the victim of Smart Money's traps.
Roux Score
The STACK grading system. Each letter (S, T, A, C, K) scores 0 or 1. A perfect score of 5/5 is an "A+ Confirmation Stack"—maximum conviction to take the trade. The Roux Score ensures you only trade your highest probability setups.
Smart Money
The institutional players (banks, hedge funds) whose large orders create the price movements retail traders see on charts. The STACK method teaches you to read their footprints and follow their accumulation, manipulation, and distribution cycles.
Structure (S in STACK)
The pattern of swing highs and swing lows on a chart. Bullish structure = higher highs and higher lows. Bearish structure = lower highs and lower lows. Structure is the first letter of STACK because everything starts here—you cannot trade without understanding the structure you're trading into.
Supply Zone
A price area where sellers previously stepped in aggressively. Price is expected to drop when it returns to this level. Think of it as a "ceiling" where selling pressure lives. Supply zones are areas where Smart Money distributed (sold) positions.
Swing High
A peak on the chart where price made a temporary high before pulling back. Identified by a high that is higher than the candles on both sides of it. Swing highs are key reference points for identifying structure and potential resistance levels.
Swing Low
A trough on the chart where price made a temporary low before bouncing. Identified by a low that is lower than the candles on both sides of it. Swing lows are key reference points for identifying structure and potential support levels.

T-W

Timeframe
The time period each candle represents on your chart. A "4-hour chart" means each candle represents 4 hours of price action. The STACK hierarchy uses Monthly, Weekly, Daily, 4-Hour, and 2-Hour. Each timeframe provides different information about institutional activity.
Top-Down Analysis
The process of starting from the highest timeframe (Monthly) and working down to the entry timeframe (2-Hour). Each level filters the next. This is how you determine if all timeframes are aligned and whether your setup has institutional backing.
Trend
The general direction price is moving. An uptrend means price is making higher highs and higher lows. A downtrend means price is making lower highs and lower lows. Trading with the trend is far more profitable than fighting it.
Trigger (T in STACK)
The specific price action event that activates your entry. You never enter without a trigger. Common triggers include: liquidity sweep + reclaim, CHoCH on a lower timeframe, displacement from a key level, and FVG fill + rejection. Trigger = 1 point on the Roux Score.
Wolf Chart 2.1
The CreoleTrades proprietary TradingView indicator used for the Confirmation step (C in STACK). It provides visual confirmation that momentum supports your trade direction. Wolf Chart 2.1 is the confirmation tool that separates high-probability setups from low-probability ones.
