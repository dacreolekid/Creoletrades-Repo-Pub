Order Blocks

Where Institutions Leave Their Footprint

1. What is an Order Block?

An order block is the last opposing candle before a strong impulsive move. This is the exact zone where institutional money placed large orders, and it represents one of the most powerful concepts in ICT (Inner Circle Trader) methodology.

Here's the critical insight: when price returns to this zone after the displacement move, those institutional orders may still be sitting there, waiting to be filled or defended. This makes the order block a high-probability reaction zone where price is likely to reverse, consolidate, or engage with institutional liquidity.

Think of it like this: an institution places a massive buy order at $100. The price dips to $100 briefly, institutions load up, then price rips to $110. Later, when price comes back down to $100, the institutions remember they still have unfilled orders and buy again. That $100 zone is your order block.

Visual: Order Block Structure — Imagine a price chart where you see a bearish candle (red) that drops from $105 to $100. Then immediately after, a strong bullish candle rips upward from $100 to $115 in one powerful move. That bearish candle from $105-$100 is a bullish order block. Institutions loaded buy orders in that zone, and the price spike upward is the displacement that confirms it.

Order blocks are not random zones—they're the institutional footprint. They tell you exactly where the smart money was active, and they show you where price is likely to interact with that liquidity again.

2. Bullish Order Blocks

A bullish order block forms when the last bearish (down) candle precedes a strong bullish displacement move. This is where institutional buyers loaded their positions before price spiked upward.

The process looks like this:

  • Price drops sharply on a bearish candle—this triggers stop losses and shakes out weak hands
  • Institutions recognize this as a buying opportunity and load large buy orders in this "fear zone"
  • Price then rips upward in a strong, impulsive move—this is the displacement
  • The bearish candle that preceded this bullish move is your bullish order block

When price later returns to this bullish order block zone, expect a reaction (bounce). Price may bounce sharply off the level, consolidate within the zone, or use it as support. The key is that bullish order blocks are magnets for price. Institutions still have orders sitting there, and they will either fill them or defend the level.

Visual: Bullish Order Block in Action — On a 4-hour chart, you see a large red candle drop from $50 to $45. The institutional buyers are positioned at $45. Immediately after, a strong green candle launches from $45 all the way to $60. That's your displacement. The red candle zone ($50 to $45) is now a bullish order block. If price comes back down to $48, expect strong buying pressure at or near the $45-$48 range.

Bullish order blocks are entry points on pullbacks, retest levels, and support zones. They're gold for traders who understand that institutions leave their orders sitting.

3. Bearish Order Blocks

A bearish order block is the mirror image of a bullish order block. It forms when the last bullish (up) candle precedes a strong bearish displacement move. This is where institutional sellers loaded their positions before price spiked downward.

The process:

  • Price rallies sharply on a bullish candle—this triggers FOMO and shakes out shorters
  • Institutions recognize this as a selling opportunity and load large sell orders in this "greed zone"
  • Price then crashes downward in a strong, impulsive move—this is the displacement
  • The bullish candle that preceded this bearish move is your bearish order block

When price returns to a bearish order block, expect selling pressure and a potential rejection. Institutions will either fill their sell orders or defend the resistance level. Bearish order blocks become resistance zones on bounces and retests.

Visual: Bearish Order Block in Action — On a daily chart, you see a large green candle rally from $100 to $115. Sellers load positions at $115. Immediately after, a powerful red candle crashes from $115 down to $85. That's your displacement. The green candle zone ($100-$115) is now a bearish order block. If price rallies back up to $110, expect strong selling pressure at or near $110-$115.

Bearish order blocks are rejection points on rallies, resistance zones, and entry points for short trades on pullbacks into the zone.

4. How to Identify a Valid Order Block

Not every candle before a move is a valid order block. There are specific criteria that separate high-probability order blocks from false signals:

  • Displacement After It: The candle must be followed by a strong, impulsive move. This should be obvious and powerful—not a weak drift or slow creep. The stronger the displacement, the more institutional presence was in that order block zone.
  • Fair Value Gap (FVG): A valid order block often creates a Fair Value Gap as part of the displacement. The FVG is the gap between the high of the opposing candle and the low of the displacement candle (or vice versa for bearish blocks). This gap represents the zone where no trading occurred—institutional orders were so strong that price gapped over it.
  • Break of Structure (BOS): The displacement should break structure in the direction of the move. If you're identifying a bullish order block, the displacement should break a previous swing low or create a higher low. This confirms institutional presence and directional conviction.
  • Unmitigated Status: The more unmitigated (untouched) an order block is, the stronger it is. A fresh order block that hasn't been retested yet carries more weight than one that's been touched multiple times.
  • Timeframe Hierarchy: Order blocks on higher timeframes carry more weight than those on lower timeframes. A 4-hour order block is stronger than a 15-minute order block because it represents more institutional activity and larger order sizes.
"The strongest order blocks are those that created a clear Fair Value Gap, broke significant structure, and have never been retested. These are institutional magnets waiting for price to return."

5. Mitigated vs Unmitigated Order Blocks

Understanding mitigation is critical to order block trading. This concept separates trade setups with low probability from those with high probability.

Unmitigated Order Blocks are fresh and untouched. Price has not yet returned to retest the zone where institutional orders were placed. These are the most powerful order blocks because the liquidity is still there, waiting to be engaged. When price finally returns to an unmitigated order block, the reaction is typically strong and reliable.

Mitigated Order Blocks are "used" order blocks. Price has returned to the zone and filled (partially or completely) the institutional orders that were sitting there. Once an order block is mitigated, it loses its significance. The institutions have either filled their orders or moved their liquidity elsewhere. A mitigated order block is no longer a high-probability level.

This is why identifying the status of an order block matters:

  • Unmitigated: Price has never returned to touch the order block zone. This is high-probability. When price reaches this level, expect a strong reaction.
  • Recently Mitigated: Price just touched the order block and created a reaction. The level is "warmed up" but still has some probability. Watch for a potential re-trade.
  • Heavily Mitigated: Price has retested the order block multiple times. The institutional liquidity is likely exhausted or moved. The level is weakening with each touch. This is lower probability.

The principle is simple: unmitigated order blocks are stronger than mitigated ones. Always prioritize trading into fresh, untouched order blocks.

6. Order Block Refinement

Once you've identified an order block, the next step is refinement—narrowing down the exact zone where institutions are most likely to react.

Use the Body, Not the Wick: When marking your order block zone, use the open to close of the candle (the body), not the wick. Wicks represent brief touches by weak hands and panic sellers. The institutional orders are placed within the body of the candle. A tighter zone gives you better entries and more accurate reaction predictions.

Multi-Timeframe Refinement: On higher timeframes, you identify the overall order block structure. Then, drop down to lower timeframes and identify which specific candle within that zone shows the most displacement. That candle is your refined order block.

For example: On a 4-hour chart, you identify a bullish order block between $100-$102. Then you drop to a 15-minute chart and see that the candle from $101-$101.50 preceded the strongest upward impulse. That $101-$101.50 zone is your refined, tighter order block. This refinement gives you a more precise entry level and tighter stops.

Visual: Order Block Refinement — Imagine a 4-hour bullish order block spanning from $48 to $52. Now zoom into the 15-minute chart within that timeframe. You see that the candle that dropped from $50 to $49.50 preceded the most violent upward spike to $60. That specific candle from $50-$49.50 is your refined order block. This is your exact trading zone.

Refinement transforms a broad zone into a precise, actionable level where you can place entries and stops with surgical accuracy.

7. Order Blocks and the STACK Method

Order blocks don't exist in a vacuum. They live inside Structure—the framework we discussed in Module 2. This is the critical integration point that turns order blocks from interesting observations into executable trades.

The STACK Method Hierarchy:

  • Step 1: Identify the structure first (Module 2). Is price in an uptrend? Downtrend? Are we in the grind phase or displacement phase?
  • Step 2: THEN look for order blocks within that structure that align with your directional bias.
  • Step 3: Trade the order blocks that support the structure, not against it.

Structure Alignment is Critical: An order block that goes against the current structure is a low-probability trade. For example, if you're in a clear uptrend (higher highs, higher lows), a bearish order block at the resistance level is weak. The structure is bullish, so bullish order blocks within the uptrend structure are your high-probability setups.

Conversely, in a downtrend, bullish order blocks against the structure should be avoided. You want bearish order blocks that align with the overall downtrend structure.

Order blocks are the micro-level confirmation. Structure is the macro-level bias. Together, they create the STACK Method's power: trade order blocks that confirm the structure, and avoid order blocks that contradict it.

Key Takeaways

  • An order block is the last opposing candle before a strong impulsive move—it's where institutional money placed large orders.
  • Bullish order blocks form before upward displacement moves; bearish order blocks form before downward moves. Each type creates a reaction zone when price returns.
  • Valid order blocks must show clear displacement, create Fair Value Gaps, and break structure. Higher timeframe order blocks carry more weight than lower timeframe ones.
  • Unmitigated (untouched) order blocks are stronger than mitigated ones. Always prioritize fresh, unused levels where institutional liquidity is still waiting.
  • Order blocks live inside structure. Always identify the structure first (Module 2), then trade order blocks that align with your directional bias. Trading order blocks against the structure is low-probability.

Practice Exercise

Find a strong impulsive move on any chart you're familiar with. Look back and identify the last opposing candle before that move began. Mark that candle's open-to-close zone (the body, not the wicks). That's your order block.

Now ask yourself: Has price returned to mitigate this order block yet? If not, it's a level to watch. If it has returned, what happened? Did price bounce off it? Create a reaction? Did it get retested multiple times (becoming heavily mitigated)?

This exercise builds your pattern recognition. The more order blocks you identify and track, the faster you'll spot them in real-time trading. And the faster you spot them, the faster you'll catch high-probability reactions at institutional liquidity zones.
