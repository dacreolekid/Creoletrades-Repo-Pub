Market Structure

The S in STACK: Read structure before anything else.

What you'll learn: Market structure is the foundation of every trade. It tells you who is in control — buyers or sellers. Before you look for entries, confirm trends, or manage risk, you must read the market structure. In this module, we explore what structure is, how to identify it, and why it's the first letter of STACK.

1. What is Market Structure?

Market structure is the pattern of price action that reveals who has control of the market at any given moment. It answers one critical question: Are buyers or sellers in charge?

Before you place a single trade, you must know the answer. This is why structure is the S in STACK.

Structure is built on two fundamental concepts:

  • Swing Highs: Local peaks where price reversed and moved lower
  • Swing Lows: Local troughs where price reversed and moved higher

By comparing swing highs to swing highs and swing lows to swing lows, you determine the direction of control. The pattern never lies: price action is the truth. You don't need oscillators, moving averages, or any other indicator. The structure is all you need to know who's winning.

"Read market structure before anything else. Where is price relative to demand and supply?"

Every trade you take should begin with this question. If you can't answer it confidently, you aren't ready to enter.

2. Bullish Structure

In bullish structure, buyers are in control. The market is trending higher. You identify bullish structure by two consecutive patterns:

  • Higher Highs (HH): Each swing high is higher than the previous one
  • Higher Lows (HL): Each swing low is higher than the previous one

In a bullish market, every pullback creates a higher low before buyers push price to a new high. This pattern repeats: pullback → higher low → new high → pullback → higher low → new high. The buyers keep support higher on each cycle, proving they control the market.

Bullish structure persists until a lower low is made — breaking the pattern of higher lows. That event signals a change in control.

3. Bearish Structure

In bearish structure, sellers are in control. The market is trending lower. You identify bearish structure with:

  • Lower Highs (LH): Each swing high is lower than the previous one
  • Lower Lows (LL): Each swing low is lower than the previous one

Bearish structure is the mirror of bullish. Each rally creates a lower high before sellers push price to a new low. This repeats: rally → lower high → new low → rally → lower high → new low. The sellers keep resistance lower on each cycle.

Bearish structure breaks when a higher high is made — creating the first sign that buyers may be stepping in.

4. Break of Structure (BOS)

A Break of Structure (BOS) is when price violates the most recent swing level in the direction of the current trend. It is confirmation that the trend is continuing.

In bullish structure: A BOS occurs when price takes out (breaks above) a previous swing high. This confirms buyers are still in control and the uptrend continues.

In bearish structure: A BOS occurs when price takes out (breaks below) a previous swing low. This confirms sellers are still in control and the downtrend continues.

Why is BOS important? Because it removes ambiguity. When you see a BOS in a bullish structure, you know the trend is alive. Buyers proved they can push higher. No guessing required.

Key point: BOS = trend continuation. If you're trading with the trend, a BOS is a confirmation signal that the move isn't over yet.

5. Change of Character (CHoCH)

A Change of Character (CHoCH) is the first break in the opposite direction of the current structure. It signals a potential reversal. The word "potential" is critical here.

In bullish structure: A CHoCH is the first lower low. It breaks the pattern of higher lows and suggests buyers may be losing control.

In bearish structure: A CHoCH is the first higher high. It breaks the pattern of lower highs and suggests sellers may be losing control.

However — and this is crucial — a CHoCH is not a confirmation of reversal. It is only the first sign. The market needs further confirmation to prove the trend has truly reversed. This is why we say "Confirmation over prediction."

A CHoCH might be followed by:

  • A pullback that establishes new structure in the opposite direction (true reversal), or
  • A bounce back into the old structure (false signal, trend resumes)

You must wait for confirmation. Do not enter a trade on a CHoCH alone.

6. Internal vs External Structure

Market structure exists on every timeframe, and the relationship between timeframes is critical to understand.

External Structure (Higher Timeframe): The larger, longer-term structure. This is the primary trend. On a 4-hour chart, the external structure is the larger pattern you see zoomed out. This structure "contains" all the smaller moves.

Internal Structure (Lower Timeframe): The smaller, shorter-term structure that unfolds within the external structure. On a 15-minute chart within a 4-hour trend, the 15-minute structure is the internal structure. It builds inside the ranges created by the external structure.

Why this matters: You can trade internal structure (small swings for quick profits) while respecting external structure (the main trend). A bullish 4-hour structure is your baseline. Even if the 15-minute chart has a small bearish pullback, the overall trend is bullish. You don't fight the external structure.

This is the foundation for top-down analysis — a topic we'll dive deeper into in Module 5. For now, understand: there is always a larger structure that contains the smaller one.

7. Structure and the STACK Method

Why is structure the first letter of STACK?

Because structure answers the most important question before you do anything else: Who is in control?

Without knowing the answer, every other signal is noise:

  • A bullish candle in bearish structure is a relief rally, not a reversal signal.
  • A trend confirmation (the T in STACK) is meaningless if you don't know what trend you're in.
  • A confluence level (the A) matters only if it aligns with structure.
  • Candlestick patterns (the C) only work when structure supports them.
  • A key level (the K) is only key if it relates to the current structure.
"You don't enter a trade if you can't answer: what's the structure?"

This is the trader's first rule. Before you think about entry, before you look at timeframes, before you glance at any indicator, you read the structure. It is your foundation. Everything else is built on top of it.

Structure tells you:

  • The direction of the primary trend
  • Where buyers and sellers have control
  • Where potential reversals form (CHoCH)
  • Where trend continuations are confirmed (BOS)
  • What constitutes a "valid" swing in that context

Without structure, you're trading blind. With structure, you trade with purpose.

Key Takeaways

  • Market structure reveals who's in control: Compare swing highs and swing lows. Bullish = Higher Highs & Higher Lows. Bearish = Lower Highs & Lower Lows.
  • Break of Structure (BOS) = Trend Continuation: In bullish structure, a break above the previous swing high is a BOS. In bearish, a break below the previous swing low. BOS confirms the trend lives.
  • Change of Character (CHoCH) = Potential Reversal (Not Confirmation): A CHoCH is the first break in the opposite direction. It signals a possible reversal but requires confirmation. Wait for further price action before entering.
  • Internal vs External Structure Matters: Larger timeframes contain smaller ones. Respect the external (higher timeframe) structure; use internal (lower timeframe) structure for precision entries within that trend.
  • Structure is the Foundation of STACK: You cannot assess Trend confirmation, Confluence, Candlestick patterns, or Key levels without first understanding the market structure. Read structure first, always.

Practice Exercise: Read the Structure

Now it's your turn. Here's what to do:

  1. Pick any chart — any asset, any timeframe. (Start with a 4-hour or daily for clarity.)
  2. Mark the swing highs and swing lows — Use a pencil or your chart tool. Identify each local peak (swing high) and each local trough (swing low).
  3. Draw the structure — Connect the swings. Is the pattern bullish (HH & HL) or bearish (LH & LL)?
  4. Find the most recent BOS or CHoCH — Look at the current price action. Has price broken a swing level in the direction of the trend (BOS)? Or has it broken against the trend for the first time (CHoCH)?
  5. Label it clearly — Write "BOS" or "CHoCH" on your chart next to the event.
  6. Ask yourself: "What does this tell me about the market right now? Who is in control?"

This is your structure read — the S in your STACK. Do this exercise on three different charts. Get comfortable reading structure without any other tools. This skill will become second nature, and it will transform how you trade.
