Glossary

Every key term from The Trader's Mind, in one place and in plain English. This is your quick-reference deck—the same vocabulary you met across all eight modules, alphabetized so you can find a concept fast and dedupe the language in your own notes. Start typing in the search box to filter the cards.

Accountability
Owning your decisions and results without excuses. It's the habit of measuring yourself against your own rules—and answering to a journal, a mentor, or a community when you break them.
Amygdala
The brain's threat detector. Fast and emotional, it can override rational thought to trigger instant survival reactions.
Amygdala Hijack
When the emotional brain overrides the thinking brain. In trading, it's the panic close, the revenge click, the frozen stare.
Analysis Paralysis
Freezing because you're chasing certainty that never comes. So much input piles up that you miss the trade or talk yourself out of a valid plan.
Anchoring
Latching onto one number—your entry price, a recent high—and judging everything against it. The anchor distorts decisions even when the market has clearly moved on.
Break-Even Bias
The urge to hold a losing trade until it returns to your entry, just to avoid the pain of being wrong.
Calibration
How well your confidence matches reality. A calibrated trader who says "60% chance" is right about 60% of the time—no overclaiming, no false modesty.
Capitulation
The moment of emotional surrender—dumping a position in despair or abandoning your plan after a string of losses. It usually happens at the worst possible price.
Checklist
A short, fixed list of conditions a trade must meet before you click. It moves the decision out of the heat of the moment and into a process you built while calm.
Circuit Breaker
A personal hard stop—a rule that pulls you out of the market after a set loss, a set number of trades, or a tilt warning. It protects the account from your worst moments.
Cognitive Load
The mental bandwidth a task consumes. Under stress it shrinks, which is why you "forget" your plan mid-trade.
Confirmation Bias
Seeing only the evidence that supports the trade you already want. You zoom into the timeframe that agrees with you and ignore the one that doesn't.
Consistency
Doing the same disciplined process trade after trade, regardless of the last outcome. It's what turns a scattered edge into a reliable one.
Decision Fatigue
The drop in decision quality after making many choices. Late in a session your judgment is worn down, which is when off-plan trades creep in.
Disposition Effect
The proven pattern of selling winners early and holding losers too long—loss aversion in live trading.
Dopamine
The brain's anticipation chemical. It spikes hardest when you might win and don't know for sure—the fuel behind the slot-machine pull of overtrading.
Drawdown
A decline in your account from a recent peak. Drawdowns are normal and survivable—managing your reaction to them matters more than the number itself.
Edge
A repeatable reason your trades make money over a large sample. An edge lives in the process and the math, not in any single trade.
Emotional Governance
The system of rules and routines you use to keep emotion from driving your decisions. Not erasing feelings—giving them a process to answer to.
Environment Design
Shaping your physical and digital space so the disciplined choice is the easy one—clean desk, alerts off, plan in view, distractions out of reach.
Euphoria
The dangerous high after a big win or a hot streak. It quietly inflates your size and lowers your standards right before the give-back.
Expectancy
The average amount you can expect to win or lose per trade, given your win rate and your average win versus average loss. Positive expectancy over time is the whole game.
Fight-or-Flight
The body's automatic stress response—adrenaline, tunnel vision, urgency. Useful against predators, dangerous against price action.
FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)
The impulse to chase a move you didn't plan because you can't stand watching it go without you. It throws structure out the window and usually buys the top.
Gambler's Fallacy
Believing a streak is "due" to reverse—that after several losers a winner must be next. Independent trades have no memory; the market owes you nothing.
HALT (Hungry/Angry/Lonely/Tired)
A quick self-check before you trade. If you're Hungry, Angry, Lonely, or Tired, your judgment is compromised—fix the state first or step away.
Heuristic
A mental shortcut your brain uses to decide fast. Often handy, but in markets these shortcuts quietly become the biases that cost you money.
Identity-Based Habits
Habits anchored to who you are, not just what you want—"I'm a trader who follows his plan." When the rule is part of your identity, discipline stops feeling like a fight.
Loss Aversion
The tendency to feel losses about twice as intensely as equivalent gains. The engine behind cutting winners and holding losers.
Max Daily Loss
A pre-set dollar or percentage limit that ends your trading day once hit. It caps a bad day before it turns into a blown account.
Outcome Bias
Judging a decision only by how it turned out. A winning trade taken off-plan was still a bad decision—and rewarding it teaches the wrong lesson.
Overtrading
Taking more trades than your edge or your account supports—often driven by boredom, the dopamine pull, or revenge. Activity is not productivity.
Position Sizing
Deciding how much to trade so a single loss stays within your plan. Correct sizing is what keeps one bad trade from doing lasting damage.
Post-Market Review
A deliberate look back after the session—what you did well, where you went off-plan, what to adjust. This is where most of the real learning actually happens.
Pre-Market Routine
The fixed sequence you run before the session—levels, bias, plan, and a state check. It puts your thinking brain in charge before the first candle prints.
Probabilistic Thinking
Treating every trade as one outcome in a distribution, not a verdict on your skill. You think in odds and samples, not in certainties.
Process vs Outcome
Separating the quality of your decision from the result of a single trade. You can do everything right and still lose—so you grade the process, not the P&L.
R-Multiple
A trade's result expressed in units of the risk you took. Risk $100 to make $300 and you booked a +3R winner—a clean way to compare trades regardless of size.
Recency Bias
Letting your last few trades dominate your judgment—turning timid after losses or reckless after wins. The recent past feels louder than it deserves to be.
Regret Aversion
Making choices mainly to avoid the sting of future regret—skipping a valid setup because the last one stung, or chasing one to avoid missing out. Fear of regret, not the plan, drives the click.
Revenge Trading
The impulse to jump back in right after a loss to "get even." It ignores the plan and turns one red trade into a red day. The cure is a mandatory pause.
Risk Per Trade
The fixed amount you're willing to lose on any single trade, set in advance. Capping it—often around 1% of the account—is what guarantees survival through a losing streak.
Sample Size
The number of trades you're judging an edge by. A handful means nothing; only a large sample tells you whether your process actually works.
Sizing to Sleep
Trading small enough that an open position never costs you a night's rest. If a trade keeps you up, your size is too big—not the market's fault.
State Management
Actively managing your physical and emotional state—sleep, food, breathing, breaks—so you sit down to trade in a clear, steady frame.
Sunk Cost Fallacy
Letting money already lost drive the next decision. The capital is gone; the only question is what the chart says now. Loss aversion makes the sunk cost scream the loudest.
Tilt
An emotional spiral—borrowed from poker—where frustration takes the wheel and decisions fall apart. Recognizing tilt early and walking away is a skill in itself.
Trade Plan
Your entry trigger, invalidation, position size, and targets—decided and written down before you click. It moves every emotional choice out of the heat of the trade.
Trading Constitution
Your personal set of non-negotiable rules—the standards you commit to in calm and refuse to renegotiate mid-trade. Your written law for how you operate.
Trading Journal
A detailed record of every trade—entry, exit, reasoning, and emotional state. Journaling is how you see your patterns clearly and stop repeating the same mistakes.
Variable Reward Schedule
A reward pattern where payoffs are unpredictable—the same loop a slot machine runs. It's the most addictive schedule there is, and the market runs it on you all day.
Variance
The natural swing in results around your true edge. Even a winning system has losing streaks and hot streaks—variance is the noise you have to sit through.
