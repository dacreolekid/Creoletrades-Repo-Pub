Options are derivatives—contracts that give you the right to buy or sell an asset at a specific price by a specific date. They're not some exotic instrument. They're a tool. This module covers what they are, how they work, and why they matter for active traders.

What Are Options

An option is a contract between two parties. One party (the buyer) has the right to buy or sell an asset. The other party (the seller) has the obligation to fulfill that contract if the buyer chooses to exercise it.

There are two types: calls and puts.

Call Option

Gives you the right to buy an asset at a fixed price (the strike) by a fixed date (expiration). You buy a call when you're bullish.

Put Option

Gives you the right to sell an asset at a fixed price (the strike) by a fixed date (expiration). You buy a put when you're bearish.

Strike Price

The fixed price at which you can buy (call) or sell (put) the underlying asset if you exercise the option.

Expiration Date

The date the option contract expires. After this date, the option is worthless. For stocks, this is the third Friday of each month.

Key principle: You don't have to exercise the option. You can sell it anytime before expiration. Most retail traders close positions before expiration—they don't actually exercise.

Premium, Strike, and Expiration

Every option quote gives you three numbers. Know them.

Premium (The Price)

What you pay to buy an option or receive if you sell one. Premium is quoted per share, but you control 100 shares per contract. So a premium of $2.50 means $250 total. Premium decreases as expiration gets closer (unless volatility spikes).

Strike Price (The Level)

Your entry price if you exercise. For calls, lower strikes are cheaper and more "out of the money." For puts, higher strikes are cheaper and more "out of the money." Strike selection defines your risk/reward.

Expiration (The Date)

How long until the contract expires. Shorter expiration = higher theta decay (time value loss). Longer expiration = more expensive but more time for the trade to work. Most active traders use weekly or monthly expirations.

Example: You buy a call on SPY (stock price $480) with a strike of $485, expiring in 7 days, for a premium of $1.50. You pay $150 total. If SPY closes above $485 at expiration, the call has intrinsic value and you profit. If it closes below $485, your call expires worthless and you lose your $150.

Intrinsic Value vs Time Value

Option premium has two components. Understanding this is critical for exits and risk management.

Intrinsic Value

The real, mathematical value of the option right now. For a call: max(0, stock price - strike). For a put: max(0, strike - stock price). Only in-the-money options have intrinsic value.

Time Value

Everything else in the premium. It's the probability that the option will move in the money before expiration, plus volatility. Time value decreases every day (theta decay).

Example: SPY is trading at $485. You buy a $485 call for $2.00 premium. If this call is at-the-money, it has zero intrinsic value—all $2.00 is time value. Now SPY rallies to $487. The same call might now be worth $3.50. That $1.50 gain includes $2.00 intrinsic value (487 - 485) plus time value erosion.

Why this matters: Buying options means you're betting on the stock to move a lot AND fast (before time decay kills the trade). Selling options means you're betting on time decay and/or stagnation. Most retail traders lose money on long calls and puts because they ignore theta.

The Greeks: Delta, Gamma, Theta, Vega

The Greeks measure how your option's price changes based on different market conditions. You don't need to be a quant to understand them. Think of them as sensitivities.

Delta (Δ)

How much your option price moves when the stock moves $1. A delta of 0.50 means your option moves $0.50 for every $1 stock move. Calls have positive delta (0 to 1). Puts have negative delta (-1 to 0). At-the-money options have delta around 0.50.

Gamma (Γ)

How much delta changes. It's the acceleration. High gamma = delta swings a lot with small stock moves. Low gamma = delta stays stable. At-the-money options have the highest gamma, especially near expiration.

Theta (Θ)

Time decay. How much value your option loses every day just from time passing (assuming stock price stays the same). Negative theta is enemy number one for buyers. Positive theta is your friend if you're selling. Short-dated options have higher absolute theta.

Vega (ν)

Volatility sensitivity. How much your option price changes if volatility (IV) increases by 1%. Longer-dated options have higher vega. Buying long-dated options is a bet on volatility expansion.

Practical take: When you buy a call, you're long delta (betting on the stock to go up), short theta (betting against time), and long vega (betting on volatility). Most retail losses come from theta working against them day after day.

Why Options Matter for Active Traders

Leverage. Control 100 shares for a fraction of the stock's cost. A $1 call option costs $100. The same 100 shares of a $100 stock costs $10,000. That's 100x leverage (in effect). Leverage cuts both ways—gains and losses are amplified.

Risk definition. Your max loss on a long call or put is the premium you paid. You can't lose more. This is clean risk.

Directional and volatility plays. You can isolate bets. Buy a call to bet on up-and-volatile. Buy a put to bet on down-and-volatile. Sell to bet on stagnation or volatility contraction.

Smart money signaling. Institutions use options flows to build or unwind positions before moving the stock. When you learn to read options chains and flows, you see what smart money sees. This ties directly into STACK Method trading.

Hedging and income. Sell covered calls to generate income on stocks you own. Use protective puts to cap downside. Use spreads to define risk. Options give you fine control.

Key Takeaways

  • A call gives you the right to buy at a fixed price; a put gives you the right to sell.
  • Premium is what you pay for the option; strike is your buy/sell price; expiration is when it expires.
  • Premium has two parts: intrinsic (real value) and time (probability + volatility).
  • Delta is how much your option moves; theta is how much time decay costs; vega is volatility sensitivity; gamma is acceleration.
  • Options give leverage, defined risk, and a window into smart money positioning.

Practice Exercise

Open a broker platform and pull up an options chain for a stock you trade. Pick a call and a put at the money (ATM). Write down their premiums, deltas, and theta. Now, move 5% in either direction—mentally price the options using delta as your guide. See how delta changes (gamma). This is how real options pricing works. Do this on 3 different underlyings and you'll have muscle memory.

Delta is your option's sensitivity to stock price moves. A delta of 0.60 means the option moves $0.60 for every $1 the stock moves. It's the most important Greek for directional traders.

The change in option value if volatility changes.
Time decay—how much value the option loses every day from time passing alone.

Theta is time decay. If an option has a theta of -0.05, it loses $5 per day (0.05 × 100 shares) just from time passing, assuming the stock doesn't move. This is why buying short-dated options is challenging—theta works against you every single day.

5. Why do active traders use options instead of stock?

Options give you 100x effective leverage (control 100 shares for less capital), defined max loss (the premium paid), and visibility into institutional activity through options flows. These are the three reasons traders choose options.
