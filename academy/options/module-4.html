Knowledge means nothing without a system. This final module teaches you to build a repeatable options playbook: position sizing, risk management, entry/exit rules, and core strategies. This is how you go from understanding options to profiting with them.

Position Sizing: Protecting Your Account

The difference between a good trader and a broke one is position sizing. You can be right on direction and still blow up if you risk too much.

The 2% Rule

Risk no more than 2% of your account on a single trade. If you have $10,000, max loss per trade is $200. If your option premium is $2.00 and you buy 1 contract ($200), that's one contract max. If you want more size, you need a bigger account or smaller risk per share.

Scaling Into Positions

Don't buy all 5 contracts at once. Buy 2 at entry, then 2 more on confirmation, then 1 at breakout. This is scaling. It reduces your average cost and lets you add to winners. Retail traders buy everything at once and panic-sell on pullbacks. Pros scale.

Account Based, Not Dollar Based

Size based on account size, not absolute dollars. A $2 move on one contract (which costs $2,000) is a 1% account gain for a $200k account but a 50% gain for a $4k account. Match your position to what you can afford to lose without panic.

Risk Management: Your Stop Loss and Exit

Before you enter, know exactly where you're wrong. This is the stop loss. Before you're up, know where you're taking profits. This is the exit.

Structural Stop Loss

Set your stop loss just beyond the structural level you're trading (order block, breaker block, etc.). If you're long a call at a demand zone and the zone breaks structurally, your thesis is broken. Exit immediately. Don't "wait for a reversal."

Profit Targets (Multi-Exit)

Never hold one contract to expiration hoping. Use three exits: (1) Take 50% at 50% profit, (2) Take 25% at 100% profit, (3) Let the last 25% run to expiration or your structural exit. This locks in gains and lets winners run.

Time Stop

If the trade hasn't moved after 2-3 days, exit. Theta is eating you alive. The market is saying "not interested." Don't be stubborn. The next trade is always coming.

Entry Rules: When to Pull the Trigger

These are your rules. Stick to them mechanically. No emotions.

  • Structure confirmed: You have identified a demand or supply zone on the chart.
  • Flow confirmed: Options activity aligns with your bias (calls if bullish, puts if bearish).
  • Liquidity confirmed: The contract has tight spread (<$0.05), volume >1,000, OI >10,000.
  • Price confirmation: Stock has either touched the zone or is setting up to (breakout or confirmation candle).
  • Position sizing: You've calculated max loss (2% rule) and number of contracts.

Example setup: SPY breaks above a supply zone at $485 with bullish options flow (calls accumulating). Calls are liquid, ATM, bid-ask tight. You're right that this is the setup. You buy 2 contracts at $485, stop loss at $483 (below the zone). That's your entry.

Core Options Strategies

Long Call

Buy a call when bullish. You pay premium (theta costs you daily). Use tight stops. Exit at structural breaks or profit targets. Best for strong bullish conviction with expected move.

Long Put

Buy a put when bearish. Same logic as long call but opposite direction. Theta is your enemy. Strong conviction and defined structural stops required.

Bull Call Spread

Buy a call, sell a call at higher strike. Reduces cost (theta works less against you). Limits max profit. Good for defined risk on smaller accounts. Wider spreads on illiquid contracts make this costly.

Bear Put Spread

Sell a put, buy a put at lower strike. You collect premium and have defined risk. High capital requirement. Use only if you're short-term bearish or neutral. Theta works FOR you.

For this course: Master long calls and puts first. Once you're consistently profitable with those, add spreads. Spreads are more complex and carry execution risk. Don't overcomplicate.

Building Your Process: The Playbook

This is how you trade every single day, no shortcuts:

Pre-Market (30 minutes before open)

Review your watchlist. Identify key support and resistance levels using STACK structure. Identify breaker blocks and order blocks where institutional repositioning might happen. Make notes on which direction you're biased (bullish, bearish, neutral).

Market Open to 2 hours (Active trading window)

Watch for structural touches. When price hits a demand or supply zone, check options flow. Are calls or puts being accumulated? Does flow align with your structural bias? If yes, set a limit buy order at the zone with your pre-calculated position size. Wait for the candle to confirm.

After Entry (Risk management)

Place stop loss immediately. Set profit target alerts. Check position daily. Manage trailing stops if you're holding winners. Exit at your targets or structural breaks, whichever comes first. Log the trade: entry, exit, P&L, what worked, what didn't. This journal is gold.

Key Takeaways

  • Never risk more than 2% of your account on a single trade. Position size everything.
  • Know your stop loss before you enter. Use structural breaks as your exit.
  • Profit targets: 50% at 50% gain, 25% at 100% gain, 25% to expiration/structural exit.
  • Entry: Structure + Flow + Liquidity + Price Confirmation. All four must align.
  • Start with long calls and puts. Once profitable, add spreads.
  • Keep a journal. Track what works and what doesn't. Improve daily.

Practice Exercise

Write down your playbook. On a single page: (1) Your position sizing rule (2% max risk). (2) Your entry rules (structure + flow + liquidity). (3) Your exit rules (profit targets and stop loss). (4) Your core strategy (long calls? spreads?). (5) Your journal format (what you'll track). Print it. Tape it to your monitor. Trade from it every day for 30 days. This discipline separates professionals from amateurs.
