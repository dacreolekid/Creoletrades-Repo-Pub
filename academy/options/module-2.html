The options chain is the raw data sheet for all available contracts on an underlying. It looks intimidating at first, but it's just columns of numbers. This module teaches you to read it like a pro: identify liquidity, spot mispricings, and find contracts worth trading.

The Options Chain: What You're Looking At

An options chain is a table showing all available contracts for a given expiration date. Each row is one contract (one strike price). Columns show bid, ask, last trade, volume, open interest, IV, and the Greeks.

Example layout (SPY 485 Call, March 29 expiration):

Strike: 485 | Bid: $2.40 | Ask: $2.50 | Last: $2.45 | Volume: 4,200 | Open Interest: 120,000 | IV: 18.5% | Delta: 0.58 | Theta: -0.08

That's the meat. You don't need to memorize every column. You need to know four things: bid-ask spread, volume, open interest, and moneyness (ITM/ATM/OTM).

Bid-Ask Spread: The Cost of Entry

The bid is what buyers are offering. The ask is what sellers are demanding. The spread between them is your cost to enter a trade.

Wide Spreads = Lower Liquidity

If bid is $2.40 and ask is $2.50, the spread is $0.10 (or 4% of the mid-price). This is wide. You're losing money on entry, and more on exit. Avoid trading contracts with wide spreads.

Tight Spreads = Liquid Contract

If bid is $2.46 and ask is $2.48, the spread is only $0.02. This is tight and signals high liquidity. You can get in and out cleanly without slippage destroying your P&L.

Avoid the Tails

Deep out-of-the-money (far from current price) and deep in-the-money contracts have wide spreads and low volume. Trade near-term, near-money contracts where spreads are tight.

Rule of thumb: If the spread is wider than $0.05 (or 1% of the mid-price), skip it. Life is too short to bleed money to market makers.

Volume and Open Interest: Liquidity Signals

Volume and open interest are different. Know the difference.

Volume

How many contracts traded today (or in the last trading session). High volume = lots of people bought and sold today. This signals current interest. Look for volume above 1,000 contracts.

Open Interest

The total number of contracts outstanding (not yet closed or exercised). A contract added to OI when opened, subtracted when closed. High OI = this contract has history and traders watching it.

What to look for:

  • Volume + Open Interest both high = liquid contract, good to trade.
  • High OI, low volume = institutional holdings, less active.
  • Low OI and low volume = illiquid, avoid.

Example: A call with 500 volume and 50,000 OI tells you smart money established positions here long ago, but not many are trading it today. A call with 15,000 volume and 8,000 OI tells you this is the hot contract—everyone is piling in today.

ITM, ATM, OTM: Where You Are vs. The Strike

Every contract falls into one of three categories based on its relationship to the current stock price.

In The Money (ITM)

The contract has intrinsic value. For a call: stock price > strike. For a put: stock price < strike. ITM contracts are more expensive and move 1:1 with the stock (delta close to 1.00 for calls, -1.00 for puts).

At The Money (ATM)

Stock price = strike (or very close). ATM contracts have the most time value and highest gamma. They're the most sensitive to moves and decay fastest. Most liquid. ATM is your sweet spot for trading.

Out The Money (OTM)

No intrinsic value. For a call: stock price < strike. For a put: stock price > strike. OTM contracts are cheaper, have low delta, and decay faster (theta is your enemy). They're lottery tickets—high probability of expiration worthless.

What to trade: ATM or slightly out-of-money. ATM contracts are most liquid. Slightly OTM (one or two strikes away) offer better odds than deep OTM while staying liquid.

How to Read an Options Chain: Step by Step

Put it all together. Here's your checklist when you open an options chain:

Step 1: Find the Current Stock Price

Locate it at the top of the chain. This is your reference point. Everything else is relative to this price. Example: SPY is trading at $485.20.

Step 2: Scan for Tight Bid-Ask Spreads

Look for contracts where the bid-ask spread is tight (under $0.05 or 1%). These are the trades worth considering. Mark them. Discard anything wide.

Step 3: Check Volume and Open Interest

Among the tight spreads, filter for contracts with volume above 1,000 and open interest above 10,000. This combo tells you the contract is liquid and watched by professionals.

Step 4: Identify Moneyness

Is this contract ATM, slightly ITM, or slightly OTM? Plot its position on the chain. ATM is safest for beginners. Slightly OTM offers better risk/reward.

Key Takeaways

  • Bid-ask spread is your trading cost. Keep it under $0.05 or 1% of mid-price.
  • High volume + high open interest = liquid contract worth trading.
  • ATM and slightly OTM contracts are most liquid. Trade these, not the deep OTM lottery tickets.
  • Moneyness (ITM/ATM/OTM) tells you how much intrinsic value a contract has and how delta will behave.
  • Always scan the chain for the liquid contracts first. Skip the rest.

Practice Exercise

Open your broker and pull up an options chain for a highly liquid stock (SPY, QQQ, or a popular ticker). Apply the four-step process: (1) Note the current stock price. (2) Find three contracts with bid-ask spreads under $0.05. (3) Check their volume and OI. (4) Identify if each is ATM, ITM, or OTM. Do this on three different expirations. You'll develop the muscle memory to spot liquid contracts in seconds.
