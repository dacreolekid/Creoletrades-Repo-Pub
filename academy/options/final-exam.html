STACK Academy Final Exam

20 cumulative questions covering all modules. No second chances per question. Click your answer.

1. What is the main advantage of trading options instead of stock?

Leverage (control 100 shares for less capital), defined risk (max loss is the premium), and visibility into institutional positioning.
Options always move further than stock.
Options never expire.
Options require no capital.
These three reasons are why active traders choose options: leverage, defined risk, and institutional signal visibility through flow.

2. What does delta approximately tell you?

The time value remaining in the option.
How much the option price moves for every $1 stock move, and the probability the option expires in-the-money.
The volatility of the underlying stock.
The cost of the option.
Delta is directional sensitivity and approximates probability. A 0.65 delta call roughly has a 65% chance of expiring ITM and moves $0.65 for every $1 stock move.

3. Why is theta (time decay) the biggest enemy for option buyers?

Because every day you hold, time value erodes regardless of stock price movement. You're fighting the clock.
Theta only affects puts.
Theta increases your profit.
Theta is not relevant to traders.
Theta decay accelerates near expiration. If you buy a short-dated option and the stock doesn't move, you lose money from time passing alone. This is why you need fast moves or longer expirations.

4. What does a tight bid-ask spread on an options contract indicate?

The option is about to expire.
High liquidity. You can enter and exit without slippage destroying your P&L.
The option is out-of-the-money.
Volume is low.
Tight spreads mean market makers are comfortable buying and selling frequently. This is liquid. Aim for spreads under $0.05 or 1% of mid-price. Wide spreads = illiquid, skip it.

5. How do volume and open interest differ?

Volume is contracts traded today; open interest is total contracts still outstanding (not yet closed).
They are the same thing.
Open interest is only for puts.
Volume can never be higher than open interest.
High OI shows historical accumulation. High volume shows today's activity. Look for both above 1,000 and 10,000 respectively. One without the other tells a different story about the contract.

6. If SPY is at $480 and you see a $485 call, what is its moneyness?

In-the-money.
At-the-money.
Out-of-the-money.
Cannot be determined.
For a call, OTM means stock < strike. $480 < $485, so it's OTM. Zero intrinsic value, all premium is time value. Decays faster than ATM.

7. What is unusual options activity (UOA) a signal of?

Retail traders having fun.
Institutional positioning. Sweep orders, block trades, or volume spikes 5-10x normal indicate smart money.
The stock is crashing.
Options are about to expire.
UOA is how institutions telegraph positions before moving the stock. A surge in call buying, IV expansion, and buying at the ask = bullish conviction. Reading this is your edge.

8. What does a sweep order indicate about the trader?

They are hesitant and testing the market.
They are aggressive and confident. They want size NOW, regardless of price.
They are market making.
They are closing a position.
Sweeps execute across exchanges simultaneously to minimize display. This is institutional conviction. Bullish sweep (buying calls) or bearish sweep (buying puts) tells you the direction they expect.

9. How do you align options flow with STACK Method chart structure?

Flow and structure are unrelated.
Identify a structural level (demand/supply zone), then check if options accumulation aligns (calls if bullish, puts if bearish). Alignment = high conviction trade.
Always trade flow regardless of chart.
Charts are irrelevant when flow is present.
Structure + Flow is the power combo. If both say the same thing, your conviction is high. If they diverge (e.g., bullish structure but put accumulation), it's a red flag. Respect the divergence.

10. What is the 2% risk rule in position sizing?

Never risk more than 2% of your account on a single trade. On a $10k account, max loss is $200 per trade.
Always use 2 contracts per trade.
The stock must move 2% for you to profit.
Only for small accounts.
The 2% rule protects your account from catastrophic losses. It's the baseline of professional risk management. Ignore this and you will blow up.

11. Where should your stop loss be set?

A fixed percentage (e.g., 10% loss).
Just beyond the structural level (demand/supply zone) you traded from. If the zone breaks, your thesis is broken.
At expiration.
You should never use a stop loss.
Structural stops are objective and tied to your trading thesis. If the structural level breaks, you're wrong. Exit immediately. Don't "wait for reversal."

12. What is the multi-exit strategy for taking profits?

Hold everything to expiration.
Sell 50% at 50% gain, 25% at 100% gain, let last 25% run to structural exit or target.
Sell everything as soon as you're profitable.
Never take profits.
Multi-exit locks in gains while letting winners run. This is professional money management. You're not being greedy; you're protecting profits while staying exposed to bigger moves.

13. What are the four requirements for entry into an options trade?

Structure (zone identified), Flow (options align with bias), Liquidity (tight spread/high volume), Price confirmation (touch or breakout).
Just a good feeling.
High volume alone.
Your gut instinct.
All four must align. If three are present but flow diverges or liquidity is poor, skip it. Discipline is the edge. The next trade is always coming.

14. What is the best strategy to start with as a beginner options trader?

Iron condors and complex spreads.
Long calls and long puts. Master these first. Once consistently profitable, add spreads.
Only sell options.
Straddles and strangles.
Long calls/puts are simple, directional, and have defined risk (max loss = premium paid). Master them before adding complexity. Spreads have execution risk and are harder to manage.

15. What does implied volatility (IV) represent?

The actual stock price movement.
The market's expectation of future volatility, implied by the option price. Higher IV = higher option premiums.
The time remaining until expiration.
The strike price divided by stock price.
IV is what the market thinks will happen (volatility-wise). High IV = expensive options. IV spikes signal uncertainty or expected big moves. IV crush (decline) hurts long options holders (vega loss).

16. What is intrinsic value?

The premium you pay.
The real, measurable value if exercised right now. For calls: max(0, stock - strike). For puts: max(0, strike - stock).
The volatility component.
The time decay rate.
Intrinsic value is objective. Only ITM options have intrinsic value. OTM options have zero intrinsic value (all premium is time value). Time value = total premium - intrinsic value.

17. What does "assignment" mean for an options trader?

When you sell your option to someone else.
When a short option is exercised against you. If you sold a call, you're assigned to sell 100 shares at the strike.
When the option expires worthless.
A homework task.
Assignment is the obligation to fulfill the contract. If you sold a call above the stock price, you could be assigned (forced to sell shares). Plan for this risk when selling options.

18. What is a "bull call spread" and when would you use it?

Selling a call to fund a stock purchase.
Buy a call, sell a call at higher strike. Used for defined risk with lower capital requirement. Limits max profit. Good for neutral-to-bullish outlook.
Always bullish, always profitable.
Used to short the market.
Bull call spread reduces theta impact and capital needed, but caps profit. The sold call premium offsets your cost. Theta works partially in your favor (it helps the short call decay).

19. If your account is $50,000 and you follow the 2% rule, what's your maximum position size on an option costing $5 per contract?

20 contracts. 2% of $50k = $1,000 max risk. $1,000 / $500 (per contract) = 2 contracts. Wait, $5 * 100 = $500/contract, so $1,000/$500 = 2 contracts.
50 contracts.
100 contracts.
No limit.
Max loss = 2% of $50k = $1,000. Option cost = $5 * 100 = $500 per contract. $1,000/$500 = 2 contracts max. Size everything this way. Never guess.

20. What is the most important thing to do before entering an options trade?

Tell others about your trade.
Set your stop loss and profit targets in advance. Define risk before you enter.
Hope the trade works out.
Check your broker's margin requirements.
Before you execute, your exit plan is locked in. Stop loss is set. Targets are defined. This is the baseline of professional trading. Emotion has no say once you're in.
