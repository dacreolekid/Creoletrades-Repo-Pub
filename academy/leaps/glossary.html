LEAP Strategy Glossary

Plain English definitions of key terms used in LEAP trading and options strategy.

LEAPs

Long-term Equity AnticiPation Securities. Options contracts with 1+ year expirations. LEAPs give traders extended time for a thesis to develop while managing capital more efficiently than owning stock outright.

Delta

The rate of change of an option's price relative to the underlying stock. Delta ranges from 0 to 1.0. A 0.75 delta call means the option price moves $0.75 for every $1 the stock moves. Higher delta = more stock-like behavior.

Theta (Theta Decay)

The daily loss in an option's value from the passage of time. Long-dated LEAPs have slower theta decay (as a percentage per day) than short-term options, making time work in your favor over 12+ months.

Vega

The sensitivity of an option's price to changes in implied volatility. If volatility increases, vega works in your favor (call values rise). LEAPs have higher vega exposure than short-term options due to longer time remaining.

In-The-Money (ITM)

For a call option, when the stock price is above the strike price. Deep ITM (e.g., stock $150, strike $130) has high delta (~0.85+) and behaves almost like owning stock. ITM options are less risky but cost more.

Out-Of-The-Money (OTM)

For a call option, when the stock price is below the strike price. OTM options are cheaper but have lower delta (~0.30) and require larger stock moves for profit. Higher leverage, higher risk.

At-The-Money (ATM)

When the stock price is near or equal to the option strike price. ATM options have delta ~0.50 and offer balanced risk/reward. They're in the middle between deep ITM and OTM.

Strike Price

The fixed price at which an option gives you the right to buy (call) or sell (put) the underlying stock. A $150 strike call on Apple is the right to buy AAPL at $150 anytime before expiration.

Extrinsic Value

The portion of an option's price that comes from time and volatility (not intrinsic value). As expiration approaches, extrinsic value decays. This is where theta loss lives. Deep ITM options have little extrinsic value left.

Implied Volatility (IV)

The market's expectation of future volatility for a stock. High IV makes options more expensive. Low IV makes them cheaper. LEAP prices are sensitive to IV changes over months/years.

Capital Efficiency

Getting more exposure for less capital. A LEAP on a $150 stock might cost $3,000 while owning 100 shares costs $15,000. LEAPs give you 100-share economic exposure with 60–70% less capital deployed.

Conviction Thesis

Your fundamental and technical reasons for expecting a stock to move in a direction over 12+ months. Examples: earnings growth trajectory, sector rotation, product cycle, insider buying. LEAPs are built for conviction trades, not guesses.

Rolling (Roll Forward)

Selling your current LEAP before expiration and buying a new LEAP with a later expiration date. Rolling allows you to extend a profitable position or reset Greeks without closing completely.

PT1/PT2/PT3 Methodology

Profit-taking framework. PT1: sell 33% at +25% gain. PT2: sell another 33% at +75% gain. PT3: let remaining 33% ride for maximum upside. Locks in capital recovery while maintaining leverage on final tranche.

Poor Man's Covered Call

Selling short-term calls against a long-term LEAP. Example: own January 2026 call, sell May 2025 call. Collect monthly premium while keeping long-term upside. Generates income on your leveraged position.

Smart Money Structure (SMC)

Institutional order blocks, supply/demand zones, and fair value gaps. Respecting SMC structure means you're trading where smart money is accumulating (demand) or distributing (supply). Key for technical confirmation of LEAP candidates.

Sector Rotation

The cyclical movement of capital from one sector to another. Tech in, energy out. Or healthcare in, tech out. LEAPs benefit from sector rotation tailwinds. A good stock in a strong sector outperforms a good stock in a weak one.

Institutional Ownership

The percentage of a company's shares held by mutual funds, pension funds, hedge funds, and other large investors. High institutional ownership (>50%) suggests analysts and large money believe in the long-term story.

Free Cash Flow (FCF)

Cash a company generates after accounting for cash outflows. Positive and growing FCF is a bullish fundamental signal. Companies with strong FCF can sustain growth narratives, reducing LEAP bankruptcy risk over 12+ months.
