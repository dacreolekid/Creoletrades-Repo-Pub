LEAPs are not just options with a longer expiration—they're a fundamentally different tool that favors time and conviction over timing. In this module, you'll understand what LEAPs are, why time decay works in your favor, and when they make sense versus other instruments. Master this foundation before you trade your first contract.

What Are LEAPs?

LEAPs are Long-term Equity AnticiPation Securities—options contracts with an expiration date of 1 year or more. Unlike traditional options (which often expire in weeks or months), LEAPs give you extended time to be right. Whether you're waiting for a company's earnings trajectory to shift, a sector rotation to play out, or a technical pattern to unfold, LEAPs compress the risk and reward into a smaller capital outlay than buying 100 shares.

Here's the key: LEAPs are 100-share contracts, just like any option. One call LEAP contract = the right to buy 100 shares at a fixed strike price anytime before expiration. One put LEAP = the right to sell 100 shares. But unlike short-dated options, LEAPs aren't racing against the clock.

Why Time is Your Friend: Theta Decay Advantage

Most traders curse theta—the daily loss in an option's value from time decay. But when you're holding LEAPs, the game flips. Here's why:

Step 1: Theta Decay is Slower

A 1-year LEAP loses value from theta decay more slowly (as a percentage of its daily cost) than a 1-month option. You have 365 days for the move to happen. You don't need to be right tomorrow—you need to be right sometime in the next 12–18 months.

Step 2: You Keep the Greeks

A LEAP deep in-the-money (like a 0.75 delta call) moves almost dollar-for-dollar with the stock. If you own a LEAP call on a $100 stock and the stock rallies to $110, your LEAP value increases by nearly $10 per share, or $1,000 per contract. You get leverage without the time pressure.

Step 3: Volatility Expansion Helps You

LEAPs have longer vega exposure. If implied volatility rises, your option becomes more valuable. In a sector rotation or bull market, vega often expands in your favor. Short-dated options? That volatility boost is minimal.

The bottom line: time is your ally with LEAPs, not your enemy. You can wait for the trade to develop without panic-selling at inopportune moments.

LEAPs vs Short-Term Options vs Stock Ownership

Short-Term Options

30–45 day contracts. Theta decay accelerates. You need the move fast. Requires active management and frequent rolling. Best for directional bets that play out in days, not months.

LEAPs (1-2 Years)

Slow theta burn. Gives time for conviction trades. Lower capital outlay than owning stock. Can roll forward if your thesis extends. Best for bullish/bearish trades with long-term conviction.

Stock Ownership

No theta decay. Full dividend exposure. Unlimited upside. Requires full capital commitment (100 shares = significant cash). Best when you're planning multi-year holds and want dividends.

Cost Basis Comparison: 100 Shares vs 1 LEAP Contract

This is where the capital efficiency argument shines. Let's say you're bullish on a $150 stock:

Own 100 Shares

Capital required: $15,000 (for 100 shares at $150).

Upside: $100 profit per $10 move (unlimited ceiling).

Downside: Full capital at risk. You hold the bag if earnings miss.

Own 1 LEAP Call (Strike $150, 18 Months)

Capital required: ~$3,000–$5,000 for the contract premium (scenario-dependent).

Upside: Same $1,000 per $10 move (capped at strike).

Downside: Limited to premium paid if the stock doesn't move.

The math: With a LEAP, you control the same 100-share economic move but deploy 67–80% less capital. That freed-up capital can work in other positions or sit as dry powder for average-downs.

When LEAPs Make Sense

  • You have strong bullish or bearish conviction on a company, sector, or trend but no specific timeframe
  • You believe a multi-quarter narrative will play out (earnings beats, product launches, sector rotation)
  • You want capital efficiency—control 100 shares with 60–70% less cash
  • You're willing to manage the position over months (rolling, taking profits, adjusting)
  • You want to limit downside risk to the premium paid (not the full stock price)
  • You're building a portfolio of levered positions across multiple tickers or sectors

When LEAPs Don't Make Sense

LEAPs aren't always the answer. Avoid them if you:

  • Need the move to happen in days or weeks (use short-dated options instead)
  • Want dividend exposure (stocks are your tool)
  • Need high optionality and frequent trading (short-term spreads are better)
  • Are trading low-liquidity or low-volume stocks (LEAP bid-ask spreads can be brutal)
  • Don't have conviction and are guessing on direction

Key Takeaways

  • LEAPs are options with 1+ year expirations—theta decay works slower and in your favor over longer timeframes
  • Control 100 shares with 60–70% less capital outlay than owning stock
  • Time is your ally. You don't need to be right tomorrow, just within your holding period
  • LEAPs are best for multi-quarter conviction trades—sector rotations, earnings trajectories, multi-year trends
  • Downside is limited to premium paid; upside scales with the stock move (deep ITM contracts)

Practice Exercise

Think of a stock you've been watching long-term. If you were bullish for the next 18 months, how much capital would you tie up owning 100 shares? Now look up a LEAP call option (18 months out, near-the-money or slight out-of-the-money). What's the difference? That's your capital freed up for other plays. This difference is the essence of LEAP strategy.
