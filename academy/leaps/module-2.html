Selecting the right LEAP candidate separates smart capital allocation from guess-and-hope. This module teaches you how to combine fundamental strength, technical setup, and option Greeks to identify trades that give you the best chance of being right over 12–18 months.

Fundamental Criteria for LEAP Candidates

LEAPs are built for bullish conviction in a company's long-term narrative. This means you're trading earnings trends, sector leadership, product cycles, and macro tailwinds—not daily noise. Here's what to look for:

Earnings Growth Trajectory

Look for companies with consistent beat-and-raise patterns. 2–3 consecutive quarters of earnings surprises signal strong operational execution. Check forward guidance and analyst consensus. Is the stock priced for the growth or below fair value?

Sector Momentum

Is your candidate in a sector that's rotating into favor? Tech uptrends, health care leadership, energy tailwinds—sector rotation can lift all boats. Compare your stock's relative strength against its peers and the broader market.

Institutional Ownership & Insider Buying

Check if institutional investors are accumulating shares. Insider buying (executives and board members) is often a bullish signal. High institutional ownership suggests analysts and fund managers believe in the long-term story.

Free Cash Flow & Balance Sheet

Strong FCF and low debt reduce bankruptcy risk over 18 months. A company with $5B in cash and growing earnings has a much higher ceiling for a LEAP than one burning cash. Balance sheet strength = convict strength.

Technical Criteria for LEAP Candidates

Even with bullish fundamentals, a stock in a downtrend or distribution pattern is a weak LEAP candidate. Pair fundamentals with technical alignment:

Step 1: Structure & Trend Confirmation

Is the stock breaking above key resistance? Are higher lows forming? Look for Smart Money Structure (SMC) alignment—supply/demand zones, fair value gaps, and order blocks. A stock respecting demand zones and breaking supply levels is showing structural strength. Don't LEAP into distribution.

Step 2: Trend Confirmation

Use moving averages (50, 200) and relative strength index (RSI). A stock trading above its 200-day MA with RSI 50–80 is in a healthy uptrend. Avoid oversold extremes (RSI <20) unless you have contrarian conviction and a clear catalyst for reversal.

Step 3: Volume & Demand Patterns

Volume breaks and reversals at demand zones signal institutional accumulation. Heavy volume on updays versus light volume on downdays shows healthy buying. Thin demand (low volume, wide spreads) = avoid.

Choosing the Right Strike Price

Strike selection makes or breaks a LEAP trade. There are three zones to understand:

Deep In-The-Money (ITM)

Strike: -$10 to -$20 below current price

Example: Stock at $150, buy $130 call. Delta ~0.85–0.95. Moves almost like owning stock. Lower leverage, higher probability. Best for capital efficiency with reduced risk.

At-The-Money (ATM)

Strike: Within $2–$5 of current price

Example: Stock at $150, buy $150 call. Delta ~0.50. Balanced risk/reward. Moderate leverage. Requires the stock to move for profit but gives more upside potential per dollar spent.

Slight Out-Of-The-Money (OTM)

Strike: $5–$15 above current price

Example: Stock at $150, buy $160 call. Delta ~0.30–0.40. High leverage, higher risk. Stock must rally for profit. Use only if your conviction is very strong and you're building a diversified portfolio.

Pro tip: For most LEAP traders, deep ITM (0.70–0.80 delta) is the sweet spot. You get 70–80% of the stock move with 60% of the capital. Leverage without roulette.

Choosing Expiration: 12–18 Months Minimum

Expiration matters more than most traders realize. Here's the math:

  • 6-month LEAP: Theta decay accelerates in months 4–6. Not enough time for multi-quarter narratives. Avoid.
  • 12-month LEAP: The goldilocks expiration. Slow theta decay. Enough time for earnings cycles, sector rotations, product launches. Your default choice.
  • 18–24 month LEAP: Maximum time decay advantage. Best for very long conviction (multi-year tech trends, sector leadership). Liquidity may be thinner (wider bid-ask).

Why longer is better: An 18-month LEAP loses ~0.14% per day to theta. A 6-month option loses ~0.25% per day. Over 180 days, that 0.11% daily difference compounds. Plus, you have 50% more time for your thesis to develop.

Delta Target Sweet Spots

Delta is the Greek that tells you how much your option moves with the stock. It ranges from 0 (far OTM) to 1.0 (deep ITM, fully stock-like). For LEAPs, here's what matters:

Delta 0.70–0.80: The LEAP Goldilocks Zone

This is where most successful LEAPs live. A 0.75 delta call moves roughly $0.75 for every $1 the stock moves. You get strong leveraged exposure with lower vega (volatility) whipsaw. If you're right, you capture most of the move. If wrong, you lose less than owning stock.

Delta 0.50–0.60: Balanced Risk/Reward

Useful when your conviction is moderate or you're in a sector with high uncertainty. You get leveraged exposure, but the option has more "theta coffin" to dig out of. Good for diversified portfolios where one position might be wrong.

Delta <0.50: High-Leverage Gambles

Use sparingly. You might own 10 positions, 2–3 with lower deltas for sector hedges or conviction plays. But betting your account on delta 0.30 calls is option roulette, not LEAP strategy.

Practical Candidate Checklist

Before you buy a LEAP, run through this checklist:

  • Earnings: 2–3 consecutive beats? Forward guidance bullish? Analyst consensus positive?
  • Sector: Is the sector rotating into favor relative to the market?
  • Structure: Is the stock respecting demand zones? Above 200-day MA? Higher lows forming?
  • Volume: Heavy accumulation at demand zones? Or thin, suspicious volume?
  • Strike: 0.70–0.80 delta. Deep ITM or ATM. No wild OTM bets unless it's 10% of position size.
  • Expiration: 12–18 months minimum. Avoid 6-month LEAPs.
  • Position Size: Is this 3–5% of your account? Can you hold it if wrong?
  • Conviction: Could you explain this thesis to a friend in 30 seconds? If not, it's not conviction.

Key Takeaways

  • LEAP candidates need fundamental strength: earnings growth, sector momentum, institutional buying, strong balance sheets
  • Technical alignment matters. Stock must be in respectable trend, respecting SMC structure, not in distribution
  • Strike selection: Target 0.70–0.80 delta (deep ITM). This gives you stock-like leverage with 60–70% less capital
  • Expiration: 12–18 months minimum. Longer = slower theta decay, more time for thesis to develop
  • Use a checklist. Discipline beats intuition

Practice Exercise

Find a stock you're bullish on for the next 18 months. Run it through the checklist: earnings trend, sector rotation, technical structure, volume patterns. Now look at the 18-month call options. Identify strikes with 0.70–0.80 delta. Calculate: How much capital for one contract? How much would 100 shares cost? What's the savings? Now ask yourself: Do I have real conviction on this thesis, or am I guessing?
