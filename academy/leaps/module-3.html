Buying a LEAP is the easy part. Managing it is where professionals separate from amateurs. This module teaches you how to lock in profits before expiration, roll LEAPs forward to extend your thesis, defend losing positions, and build a diversified LEAP portfolio across sectors.

Taking Profits: The PT1/PT2/PT3 Methodology

Many traders enter a LEAP and hold until expiration hoping for the moon. Smart traders take profits in tranches. This reduces emotional attachment and locks in winning capital. Here's the framework:

Step 1: PT1 (First Target)

When your LEAP reaches +25% profit, sell 33% of the position. Lock in the initial gain. This pays for the cost of entry and gives you free capital to deploy. Psychologically, you now own "house money."

Step 2: PT2 (Second Target)

When the stock has moved further and your remaining position is at +75% profit, sell another 33%. You've now recovered your entire original capital and locked in serious gains. One-third remains for the "lottery ticket" upside.

Step 3: PT3 (Let It Ride)

The final 33% rides until expiration or until you hit your maximum conviction target. This is where 100-baggers live—you've already recovered capital, so this is free leverage. Let the thesis play out.

Example: You buy a $150 stock's LEAP call for $3,000 (January '26 expiration, $140 strike). Stock rallies to $165. Your LEAP is now worth $3,750 (+25%). Sell 33% ($1,250), lock in your first gain. Stock rallies to $210. Your remaining 66% is worth ~$7,000 total (+75%). Sell another 33% ($1,250), you've now recovered your entire $3,000 investment. Final 33% can ride for maximum upside.

Rolling LEAPs Forward: Extending Your Position

Your thesis doesn't end when the LEAP expires. Rolling allows you to extend a profitable position into a new LEAP at a later expiration, managing costs and staying positioned.

When to Roll

Timing: 4–8 weeks before expiration. You still have extrinsic value. Rolling too early wastes remaining time value. Rolling too close to expiration gives you thin liquidity.

How to Roll

Sell your current LEAP (the near-term one) and buy a new LEAP further out (6–12 months away). The goal: recover most of the proceeds from the sale and roll into a new position at a higher strike or same strike depending on conviction.

Example Roll

Your January '26 $140 call is now in-the-money at +80%. Sell it for $8,000. Buy an October '26 $150 call for $6,000. Net credit: $2,000. You keep the gain and extend your position with capital recycling.

Why roll? Your stock thesis is intact but expiration is approaching. Instead of taking the profit 100%, rolling lets you stay leveraged with fresh Greeks and time decay on your side.

Defensive Moves: When Your LEAP Goes Against You

Not every LEAP works. When a position is underwater, you have options:

Step 1: Reassess Your Thesis

Has the fundamental story broken? Did earnings miss badly? Did the sector rotate against you? Or is this just a temporary pullback in a still-intact narrative? Emotion kills traders. Review the facts, not your P&L.

Step 2: Scale Out or Cut Loss

If the thesis is broken, sell 50% immediately and cut your losses on the rest. Don't let a -30% position become -80%. If the thesis is intact but timing is off, hold and wait. You have 12+ months. Short-term noise is not long-term damage.

Step 3: Sell Call Spreads to Lower Cost Basis

If you're holding underwater LEAPs, sell a call spread against them. This brings in premium to lower your effective cost. Example: Your $140 call is down -40%. Sell the $170 call (OTM). Collect $500. Lower your cost basis while keeping upside to $170. You've bought time.

Selling Covered Calls Against LEAPs (Poor Man's Covered Call)

A "poor man's covered call" is selling short-dated calls against your long LEAP. This generates income while you wait for your long-term thesis to play out.

  • Own 1 LEAP (long-term call, e.g., January 2026)
  • Sell a short-term call 1–2 months out at a higher strike (e.g., sell June $160 call)
  • Collect premium. If stock stays below $160, keep the premium and sell again next month
  • If stock rallies past $160, you're called away but make profit on both legs
  • This is income generation while maintaining long-term upside

Example: You own the $140 LEAP for $3,000. Stock is at $155. Sell the $165 call (2 months out) for $800. If stock stays below $165, pocket $800, roll the call again next month. Over 12 months, you could collect $3,000–$4,000 in short-term premium, essentially making your LEAP free or profitable.

Building a LEAP Portfolio: Diversification Across Sectors

A LEAP portfolio is not "put all capital into one stock." Smart traders build balanced, sectoral exposure:

Position Sizing

Each LEAP = 3–5% of account. If you have $100K, each position is $3K–$5K. This allows 15–25 positions without overleveraging. One bad pick doesn't blow you up.

Sector Allocation

Spread positions across tech, healthcare, energy, financials, industrials. Don't load up 80% tech just because it's hot. Sector rotation is real. Diversification cushions rotation risk.

Conviction Tiers

Tier 1 (High): 5% position size. Tier 2 (Medium): 4% position size. Tier 3 (Exploratory): 2–3% position size. This structure rewards conviction while managing risk on exploratory bets.

Portfolio math: If 60% of your LEAPs hit +100%, 30% hit +30%, and 10% hit -50%, your portfolio return is (0.6 × 1) + (0.3 × 0.3) + (0.1 × -0.5) = +75%. That's why diversification + conviction = wealth.

Key Takeaways

  • Use PT1/PT2/PT3 profit-taking: Take 25%, 75%, and full profits in tranches. Lock in gains, recover capital
  • Roll LEAPs forward 4–8 weeks before expiration to extend profitable positions with fresh Greeks
  • Defend losing positions: Reassess thesis, scale out, or sell call spreads to lower cost basis
  • Sell short-dated calls against LEAPs for income generation (poor man's covered call)
  • Build a diversified LEAP portfolio: 3–5% per position, spread across sectors, conviction-weighted
  • Discipline > Hope. Lock in profits, cut losses, and manage risk with position sizing

Practice Exercise

Take one LEAP position you're tracking. Map out your PT1 (+25%), PT2 (+75%), and PT3 (target) price levels. Calculate how much you'll pocket at each milestone. Now build a hypothetical 5-position LEAP portfolio: 2 high-conviction, 2 medium-conviction, 1 exploratory. Assign sector diversification. Calculate your breakeven if 60% hit 100%, 30% hit 30%, and 10% lose 50%. This is real portfolio management.
