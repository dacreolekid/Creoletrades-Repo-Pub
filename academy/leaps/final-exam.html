LEAP Strategy Final Exam

15 questions covering all three modules. This is cumulative—no second chances per question. Click your answer and read the explanation.

1. What does LEAP stand for?

Leveraged Equity Asset Positions
Long-term Equity AnticiPation Securities
Long-Entry Alternative Portfolio
Leverage Every Asset Potential
LEAPs are Long-term Equity AnticiPation Securities—options with 1+ year expirations. This full name encapsulates the philosophy: anticipation of long-term moves with leveraged capital allocation.

2. Why is theta decay an advantage for LEAP traders vs short-term option traders?

LEAPs lose theta more slowly as a percentage per day, giving you 365+ days for the move to develop
LEAPs don't experience theta decay
Short-term options are immune to theta decay
Theta only affects put options
A 1-year LEAP loses ~0.14% per day to theta. A 30-day option loses ~0.25% per day. Over 365 days, that difference compounds significantly. Time is your ally with LEAPs, not your enemy.

3. How much capital do you typically need for a LEAP vs owning 100 shares?

The same amount
60–70% less capital for the LEAP (you control the same 100 shares with less money)
LEAPs cost more than stock
Capital costs are irrelevant
This is the capital efficiency advantage. A $150 stock = $15,000 for 100 shares. An 18-month $140 call might cost $3,000–$5,000. Same economic exposure, 60–70% less capital deployed.

4. What's the ideal delta range for LEAP trades?

0.10–0.30 (far OTM)
0.70–0.80 (deep ITM)
1.0 (pure stock replacement)
Delta doesn't matter
0.70–0.80 delta is the sweet spot. You get 70–80% of stock movement with leveraged capital. Ultra-low deltas are gambling; 1.0 defeats the leverage purpose. This zone balances conviction with risk management.

5. What's the minimum recommended expiration for a LEAP?

3 months
6 months
12 months (1 year)
6 weeks is fine
12 months is the minimum. A 6-month option accelerates theta decay in the final months. You need 12+ months for multi-quarter narratives (earnings cycles, sector rotations, product launches).

6. Which of these is NOT a fundamental criterion for LEAP candidates?

Consistent earnings beats and positive guidance
Sector momentum
The stock should be down 50% from its high (more likely to bounce)
Institutional ownership and insider buying
A down 50% stock isn't automatically a LEAP candidate. You want fundamental strength, not value traps. Sector momentum, earnings growth, and institutional buying are the real signals. Cheap ≠ good.

7. What's the PT1 profit-taking milestone in the PT1/PT2/PT3 methodology?

Sell 33% when position is up +25%
Sell 33% when position is up +100%
Hold until expiration for max profit
Don't take any profits; let it ride
PT1 = +25%. You sell 33% and lock in your first gain. This reduces emotional attachment and frees up capital. Psychology matters as much as math in trading.

8. When should you roll a LEAP to a new expiration?

Immediately after buying
4–8 weeks before expiration when you still have extrinsic value
1 day before expiration
Rolling is never recommended
The sweet spot for rolling is 4–8 weeks before expiration. You still have time value, but liquidity is good. Rolling too early wastes extrinsic value; rolling too late gives thin liquidity.

9. What is a "poor man's covered call"?

Buying cheap options and holding them
Selling short-dated calls against your long-term LEAP to generate income
Avoiding options entirely and buying stock
Selling LEAPs outright
Own a January 2026 LEAP, sell a May 2025 call. Collect monthly premium. If the stock rallies past the short call strike, you keep both profits. It's income generation on your leveraged position.

10. What's the recommended position size per LEAP in a diversified portfolio?

10–20%
3–5% (depending on conviction level)
50% or more
1% maximum (too small)
3–5% per position allows 15–25 positions without overleveraging. One bad trade won't blow your account. Higher conviction = 5%, exploratory = 2–3%. Diversification beats concentration.

11. When your LEAP thesis breaks fundamentally, what's the first defensive action?

Hold and hope it bounces
Reassess the facts objectively, then sell 50% immediately if the thesis is broken
Double down to lower cost basis
Ignore the loss and focus elsewhere
Emotion kills traders. Separate feelings from facts. If earnings miss badly or sector rotates against you, the thesis is broken. Cut 50% to prevent -30% from becoming -80%.

12. Which Greek represents time decay in options?

Delta
Theta
Vega
Gamma
Theta is time decay. It's the daily loss in an option's value as expiration approaches. LEAPs have lower theta burn than short-term options, making time your ally over 12+ months.

13. What does delta 0.75 on a LEAP call mean?

For every $1 the stock moves, the option moves ~$0.75
The option will expire worthless 75% of the time
You have a 75% probability of profit
The option is 75% more expensive than stock
Delta 0.75 = the option moves roughly $0.75 for every $1 the stock moves. It behaves like owning 75 shares. This is the goldilocks zone: stock-like movement with leveraged capital.

14. Which factor is MOST important when selecting a LEAP candidate?

The option is very cheap
You have genuine long-term conviction backed by fundamentals, sector momentum, and technical structure
The stock is trending (any trend is fine)
Your friend tipped you on it
Conviction is everything. You need fundamentals (earnings growth, balance sheet), sector tailwinds, institutional buying, AND technical confirmation. Cheap options on weak stocks are cheap for a reason.

15. What's the key advantage of LEAPs over short-term options for long-conviction trades?

Lower capital requirements only
Time works in your favor (slow theta), you have 365+ days for thesis development, and capital efficiency combine
Guaranteed profit
No downside risk
LEAPs combine three advantages: capital efficiency (60–70% less deployed), slow theta decay (time is an ally), and extended timeframe (365+ days for multi-quarter narratives). This is the foundation of LEAP strategy mastery.
