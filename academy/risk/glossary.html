Risk Management Glossary

A comprehensive reference of key terms used throughout the Risk Management course. Each term is defined in plain language and cross-referenced to the module where it's covered in depth.

Risk-to-Reward Ratio

Module 1: Foundations

The relationship between the amount of capital you risk and the potential profit on a trade. A 1:3 ratio means you risk $1 to make $3. Favorable R:R ratios are essential for long-term profitability.

Stop Loss

Module 1: Foundations

A predetermined price level where you exit a losing trade to prevent further losses. Setting a stop loss is a cornerstone of disciplined risk management and defines your maximum loss per trade.

Take Profit

Module 1: Foundations

A predetermined price level where you exit a winning trade to lock in profits. Take profit targets ensure you capture gains rather than holding winners until they become losers.

Position Size

Module 1: Foundations

The number of shares, contracts, or units you trade per entry. Proper position sizing ensures your risk per trade matches your account size and trading plan, preventing catastrophic losses.

Portfolio Heat

Module 1: Foundations

The total percentage of capital at risk across all open positions. Managing heat prevents over-leveraging and ensures you never risk too much of your account on a single market move.

Max Drawdown

Module 1: Foundations

The largest peak-to-trough decline in your account balance over a specified period. Understanding your maximum drawdown helps you mentally prepare for downturns and size trades accordingly.

Risk Budget

Module 1: Foundations

The total amount of capital allocated for losses during a trading session, day, week, or month. Once your risk budget is exhausted, you stop trading until the period resets.

Expected Value

Module 1: Foundations

The average profit or loss per trade calculated from your win rate and risk-reward ratio. Positive expected value over time is the only way to achieve consistent profitability.

1% Rule

Module 1: Foundations

A foundational rule stating you should never risk more than 1% of your account on a single trade. This rule protects you from catastrophic losses and ensures survival across multiple losing trades.

Gap Risk

Module 2: Equities

The risk that a stock opens far away from the previous close, potentially gapping through your stop loss. Gap risk is highest around earnings announcements, economic data, and corporate news.

Circuit Breaker

Module 2: Equities

An automated trading halt triggered when an index drops sharply in a short time. Circuit breakers give the market time to cool during extreme moves and can trap you if you're caught wrong.

Trailing Stop

Module 2: Equities

A dynamic stop loss that follows the price upward but stays fixed when price falls. Trailing stops lock in profits while allowing winners to run, adapting to market momentum.

Hard Stop

Module 2: Equities

A stop loss order entered into your broker at market execution to guarantee exit. Hard stops execute automatically, preventing emotional hesitation during losing trades.

Mental Stop

Module 2: Equities

A stop loss you track mentally but don't enter into your broker. Mental stops require high discipline and are prone to override. Always favor hard stops over mental discipline.

Sector Correlation

Module 2: Equities

The degree to which stocks in the same sector move together. High correlation means your diversification is false; holding multiple correlated positions creates concentrated risk.

Earnings Risk

Module 2: Equities

The risk of sharp price moves when a company reports quarterly earnings. Earnings risk can be managed by reducing position size ahead of reports or avoiding earnings-week trades entirely.

Delta

Module 3: Options

The rate at which an option's price changes relative to the underlying stock price. A delta of 0.5 means the option moves $0.50 for every $1 move in the stock; delta ranges from 0 to 1 for calls.

Theta

Module 3: Options

The rate at which an option loses value as time passes (time decay). Theta accelerates as expiration approaches; selling options benefits from theta while buying options suffers from it.

Gamma

Module 3: Options

The rate of change of delta itself, measuring how delta accelerates. High gamma means your delta hedge needs constant rebalancing; gamma risk increases as options approach expiration.

Vega

Module 3: Options

The rate at which an option's price changes relative to changes in implied volatility. Long options benefit from rising volatility; short options benefit from falling volatility. Vega risk is significant in high IV environments.

ITM (In-The-Money)

Module 3: Options

An option with intrinsic value: a call with strike below the stock price or a put with strike above the stock price. ITM options have higher delta, lower vega, and higher assignment risk.

ATM (At-The-Money)

Module 3: Options

An option with strike equal to or very close to the current stock price. ATM options have delta near 0.5, peak gamma, and peak vega, making them the most volatile per price move.

OTM (Out-of-The-Money)

Module 3: Options

An option with no intrinsic value: a call with strike above the stock price or a put with strike below the stock price. OTM options have lower delta, lower cost, and zero assignment risk.

Premium

Module 3: Options

The price paid or received for an option contract, composed of intrinsic value plus time value. Premium decays over time due to theta; selling premium benefits from time decay.

Assignment Risk

Module 3: Options

The risk that an option you sold gets exercised early, forcing you to buy or sell the underlying. Assignment risk is highest for ITM short options, especially before ex-dividend dates.

Credit Spread

Module 3: Options

A spread where you sell a higher-premium option and buy a lower-premium option (same expiration, same underlying). Max profit is the credit received; max loss is limited but can exceed margin.

Max Loss

Module 3: Options

The maximum dollar amount you can lose on an options trade. For spreads, max loss equals the spread width minus premium received. Knowing max loss is critical for position sizing.

DTE (Days To Expiration)

Module 3: Options

The number of calendar days remaining until an option contract expires. Theta accelerates with lower DTE; most time decay happens in the final 2 weeks before expiration.

Pip

Module 4: Forex

The smallest unit of price movement in a currency pair, typically 0.0001 for major pairs. One pip move on 1 standard lot equals $10; pips are the building blocks of forex risk calculations.

Standard Lot

Module 4: Forex

A forex position size of 100,000 units of the base currency. A standard lot carries the highest pip value ($10 per pip for major pairs) and requires significant capital and risk management.

Mini Lot

Module 4: Forex

A forex position size of 10,000 units of the base currency, one-tenth of a standard lot. A mini lot equals $1 per pip for major pairs, making it ideal for learning and risk management.

Micro Lot

Module 4: Forex

A forex position size of 1,000 units of the base currency, one-hundredth of a standard lot. A micro lot equals $0.10 per pip for major pairs and is perfect for precise 1% rule execution.

Pip Value

Module 4: Forex

The dollar value of a one-pip move for a given lot size. Pip value scales with lot size; knowing pip value is essential for calculating risk per trade and proper position sizing.

Effective Leverage

Module 4: Forex

Your actual leverage based on position size relative to account equity, not the broker's offered leverage. A $10k account trading 1 standard lot has 10:1 leverage; prudent traders use far less.

Currency Correlation

Module 4: Forex

The degree to which two currency pairs move together. Highly correlated pairs (like EURUSD and GBPUSD) create false diversification; managing correlation prevents concentration risk.

Margin Call

Module 4: Forex

A broker's demand to deposit additional funds when your account equity falls below required margin. Margin calls force liquidation of positions at bad prices; maintaining excess margin is critical.

Liquidation Price

Module 5: Crypto

The price level where your leveraged position is automatically closed due to insufficient margin. Understanding your liquidation price is mandatory before entering any leveraged trade in crypto.

Funding Rate

Module 5: Crypto

A periodic payment exchanged between long and short traders on perpetual futures contracts. High positive funding rates indicate a crowded long market and represent a cost to your position; they can turn profitable trades negative.

Cold Storage

Module 5: Crypto

Storing cryptocurrency offline in hardware wallets or paper wallets to prevent exchange hacks. Cold storage eliminates custodial risk but requires careful key management and backup procedures.

Custodial Risk

Module 5: Crypto

The risk that an exchange or custodian loses, mishandles, or steals your crypto assets. Major exchanges have been hacked (Mt. Gox, FTX); only keep active trading capital on exchanges.

Smart Contract Risk

Module 5: Crypto

The risk that code flaws in DeFi protocols cause unintended behavior and loss of funds. New protocols are highest risk; audits reduce but don't eliminate smart contract risk.

Impermanent Loss

Module 5: Crypto

The loss in dollar value when you provide liquidity to an AMM and one asset appreciates significantly more than the other. Impermanent loss is real and must be offset by trading fees to break even.

Rug Pull

Module 5: Crypto

A scam where developers drain liquidity pools or lock holders' tokens and disappear with funds. Rug pulls are common in unverified DeFi projects; only participate in audited protocols with real teams.

Revenge Trading

Module 6: Psychology

The dangerous impulse to immediately re-enter the market after a loss to "get even." Revenge trading ignores your trading plan and leads to larger losses; the cure is a mandatory pause after hitting your daily loss limit.

FOMO

Module 6: Psychology

Fear of Missing Out—the impulse to chase a trade late because you fear missing profits. FOMO kills risk management; every trade either fits your plan or doesn't, regardless of how much money others are making.

Overtrading

Module 6: Psychology

Taking more trades than your system generates or your account size supports. Overtrading reduces win rate through poor execution and increases fees; quality over quantity always.

Drawdown Recovery

Module 6: Psychology

The psychological and mathematical challenge of recovering from losses. A 20% drawdown requires a 25% gain to break even; understanding this ratio prevents over-aggressive recovery attempts.

Trading Journal

Module 6: Psychology

A detailed record of every trade including entry, exit, reasoning, and result. Journaling enables objective analysis of your edge and prevents emotional decisions from repeating failures.

Risk-On/Risk-Off

Module 6: Psychology

Market sentiment cycles where traders either buy risky assets (risk-on) or shift to safe havens (risk-off). Understanding the macro cycle helps you adjust position sizing and hedge ratios appropriately.

Hedging

Module 6: Psychology

Taking an offsetting position to reduce risk in another position. A hedge reduces both downside risk and upside profit; hedging is insurance, not a free lunch.

Beta

Module 6: Psychology

A measure of how much an asset moves relative to the overall market. High-beta assets (like growth stocks or leveraged crypto) amplify market moves and increase portfolio volatility.

Correlation

Module 6: Psychology

The degree to which two assets move together, ranging from -1 (perfectly inverse) to +1 (perfectly together). True diversification requires low or negative correlation between holdings.
