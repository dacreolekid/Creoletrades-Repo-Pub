Forex Is Leverage on Steroids

Standard forex accounts offer 50:1 to 500:1 leverage. This means a 1% move can wipe 50-500% of your account. Let that sink in for a moment. A 1% move. That's nothing on a daily chart. And it can destroy you.

The #1 reason forex traders blow up is simple: overleveraging. They get excited by the big leverage, take positions that are too large, and one stop loss wipes them out. Leverage is a tool, not a superpower.

"Leverage is gasoline. Used right, it powers the engine. Used wrong, it burns the whole car down." — CreoleTrades

Your job is to respect the leverage and use it strategically. Not every trade deserves maximum leverage. In fact, most don't.

Pip Values & Lot Sizing

Before we talk about leverage, you need to understand pips and lot sizes. This is the foundation of forex position sizing.

Understanding Lots

Standard Lot: 100,000 units. One pip move = $10 profit/loss (for most pairs).

Mini Lot: 10,000 units. One pip move = $1 profit/loss.

Micro Lot: 1,000 units. One pip move = $0.10 profit/loss.

Pip Values by Pair

Pip values vary by currency pair. Here's how to calculate them:

Pair Standard Lot (100k) Mini Lot (10k) Micro Lot (1k)
EUR/USD $10 per pip $1 per pip $0.10 per pip
GBP/USD $10 per pip $1 per pip $0.10 per pip
USD/JPY $8-$10 per pip $0.80-$1 per pip $0.08-$0.10 per pip
AUD/USD $10 per pip $1 per pip $0.10 per pip

Lot Sizing Formula

Lot Size = (Account × Risk%) / (Stop Loss in Pips × Pip Value)

Example: You have a $5,000 account, risk 1% ($50), stop is 50 pips, pip value is $1 (mini lots).

Lot Size = $50 / (50 pips × $1) = 1.0 mini lots (or 0.1 standard lots)

Pip

Percentage in point. Usually the 4th decimal place (0.0001) for most pairs, 2nd for JPY pairs.

Standard Lot

100,000 units of the base currency. Standard position size in forex.

Mini Lot

10,000 units of the base currency. Easier for managing risk on smaller accounts.

Micro Lot

1,000 units of the base currency. Good for learning and testing strategies.

Leverage Management

Just because you CAN use 100:1 leverage doesn't mean you should. Most retail traders shouldn't use more than 10:1 effective leverage. Here's why: high leverage = high volatility in your account equity. One bad week and you're done.

Effective Leverage = Total Position Value / Account Equity

Example: You have $5,000. You buy 1 standard lot of EUR/USD (100,000 units × $1.08 = $108,000 exposure). Effective leverage = $108,000 / $5,000 = 21.6:1. That's too high.

1Check Your Effective Leverage
Calculate total position value by multiplying lot size × current exchange rate × units. Then divide by account equity. If it's above 10:1, you're over-leveraged. Period.
2Adjust Lot Size to Stay Safe
Use the lot sizing formula above to ensure your position size keeps effective leverage under 10:1. This might mean trading mini or micro lots if you have a smaller account. That's fine. Better to be small and consistent than big and broke.
3When Higher Leverage Is Acceptable
If you have a micro account ($100-$500) and use micro lots with tight 10-20 pip stops, higher leverage is acceptable because your absolute dollar risk is small. A $100 micro account with 100:1 leverage and a 20-pip stop risks $2. That's fine. But don't kid yourself—you're still at high risk of ruin.

The Rule: Keep effective leverage under 10:1 until you've consistently profited for 1+ years. Then you can consider higher leverage if you understand your risk.

Currency Pair Correlation

This is where most traders make a critical mistake. They think they're diversified when they're actually doubling down on the same bet.

EUR/USD and GBP/USD are positively correlated. They move together most of the time. If you go long both pairs, you're not diversifying—you're effectively doubling your USD short exposure.

EUR/USD and USD/CHF are negatively correlated. They move opposite. If you go long EUR/USD, you're effectively long EUR and short USD. If you go short USD/CHF, you're short USD and long CHF. These DON'T cancel out. You're taking on different risks.

Common Forex Correlations

Pair 1 Pair 2 Correlation What It Means
EUR/USD GBP/USD Positive (+0.80) They move together. Long both = double USD short.
EUR/USD USD/CHF Negative (-0.85) They move opposite. You're taking different bets.
AUD/USD NZD/USD Positive (+0.75) Commodity-linked currencies move together.
EUR/USD USD/JPY Negative (-0.65) Risk sentiment drives these opposite directions.

Risk Implication: If you're trading multiple pairs, check their correlation. If they're positively correlated and you're on the same side, you're taking concentrated risk. If you're on opposite sides, make sure you're intentionally hedging, not just hedging accidentally.

Session Volatility & News Risk

Forex volatility isn't random. It clusters around trading sessions and economic news. Understanding this is critical for position sizing and timing.

Trading Sessions

Sydney Session (22:00-06:00 UTC): Low volatility. Good for scalping tight ranges.

Tokyo Session (00:00-09:00 UTC): Medium volatility. Asia trading. JPY pairs spike here.

London Session (08:00-17:00 UTC): High volatility. The big money trades. Trends develop.

New York Session (13:00-22:00 UTC): Very high volatility. Most volatile overlaps (London-NY) happen 13:00-17:00 UTC.

Overlap Sessions (13:00-17:00 UTC): Peak volatility. Big moves happen here.

Economic News Events

Certain economic releases cause massive spikes. NFP (Non-Farm Payroll), FOMC decisions, ECB announcements, and central bank rate decisions can move pairs 50-100+ pips in seconds.

Strategy: Reduce size or close positions before high-impact news. It's not worth the risk of a gap or spike. Some traders trade the news, but that's a different skill. For most swing traders, it's easier to just step aside.

Check the economic calendar on Forexfactory.com or your broker's calendar before trading. Identify high-impact events (red events) and plan your exits accordingly.

Key Takeaways

  • Leverage is gasoline. It amplifies both profits and losses. Overleveraging is the #1 cause of account blowups.
  • Master lot sizing using the formula: Lot Size = (Account × Risk%) / (Stop × Pip Value). Use mini or micro lots if needed.
  • Keep effective leverage under 10:1. Calculate it regularly. If you're over-leveraged, reduce position size immediately.
  • Understand currency pair correlation. Buying EUR/USD and GBP/USD isn't diversification—it's a doubled bet.
  • Volatility clusters around London and New York sessions and major economic news. Reduce size or exit before high-impact events.
  • Use the economic calendar. Check it before every trading session. Plan your exits around scheduled news.

Practice Exercise

Open your forex platform right now and do this:

  1. Check what leverage you're currently authorized for. Write it down. Is it 50:1? 100:1? 500:1?
  2. Look at any open positions you have. For each one, calculate the effective leverage: (position value in USD / account equity).
  3. Is effective leverage under 10:1? If not, you need to reduce position size.
  4. Check if any of your pairs are correlated. Are EUR/USD and GBP/USD both long? That's double USD short exposure. Is that intentional?
  5. Open the economic calendar. What high-impact events are scheduled this week? Plan your exits around them.

This is how you stay alive in forex. Not by hoping to win, but by systematically managing risk.
