The Invisible Risks

Technical risk is only half the game. The other half is psychological: revenge trading, FOMO, overtrading, refusing to take a loss. This module ties everything together — your portfolio is a system, and you are the weakest link in that system until you learn to manage yourself.

You can have perfect position sizing, perfect entries, perfect exits — and blow up your account in a single day by breaking your own rules. The rules don't fail. You do. The question is: will you catch yourself before you do?

Drawdown Management

Define maximum drawdown. The math of recovery is brutal and asymmetric: losing 10% requires 11% to break even. Losing 50% requires 100%. This is why small losses matter more than big wins. This is why preventing disasters matters more than chasing riches.

Think about it differently: if you make 10 trades and win 7 of them, you're still not profitable if the 3 losses are too big. One 50% loss wipes out five 10% wins. The math is merciless. This is where drawdown limits save your life.

Daily, Weekly, Monthly Limits

1 Daily Max Loss
Set a rule: "I stop trading when I've either (a) done 3 trades, or (b) lost 3% of my account — whichever comes first." Once you hit the limit, you stop. Period. No exceptions. No "one more trade to get it back." That's the path to 10% losses becoming 50% losses.
2 Weekly Drawdown
Set a weekly limit. If you're down 5-7% for the week, you stop. Come back Monday with a fresh mind and fresh capital. A bad week is a learning week, not a capital destruction week.
3 Monthly Limit
If you lose 10-15% in a month, you're done for the month. Close out positions, step back, and review. That drawdown tells you something is broken in your system. Find it before you lose another 10%.

The Rule: When you hit your limit, you stop trading. Not tomorrow. Now. This isn't a suggestion. It's a circuit breaker. And it will save your account more than any perfect entry ever will.

Correlation Across Asset Classes

You might be long SPY calls, long EUR/USD, and long BTC — and think you're diversified. In a risk-off event, all three drop simultaneously. You're not diversified. You're triple-leveraged to risk-on sentiment.

This is the invisible risk. When correlation breaks down, all your "hedges" move in the same direction. It happens every major market drawdown. March 2020. September 2022. Always the same pattern: when risk goes off, everything correlated to risk-on goes down.

Correlation

How two assets move together. Perfect correlation = 1.0 (move together). Zero correlation = 0 (move independently). Negative correlation = -1.0 (move opposite).

Risk-On / Risk-Off

Risk-on: Investors buy risky assets. Risk-off: Investors flee to safety. In risk-off events, almost all risky assets drop together.

Diversification

True diversification means holdings that move differently. Stocks + bonds. Equities + gold. Equities + USD. Not stocks + crypto + forex (all risk-on correlated).

Hedging

Owning assets that gain when others lose. Gold, USD, or long VIX when you're long equities. Real hedges have negative correlation.

Beta

How much an asset moves with the overall market. Beta of 1.0 = moves with market. Beta > 1.0 = more volatile than market. Beta < 1.0 = less volatile.

How to Check Real Diversification

1 Identify Your Risk Exposures
Long SPY calls = risk-on / equities. Long EUR/USD = risk-on / carry trade. Long BTC = risk-on / alternative assets. All three are risk-on. You're not diversified.
2 Test in Past Drawdowns
Look back at March 2020. What was your portfolio's correlation? Did SPY and BTC both drop 30%? If yes, they're correlated to risk-off. A real hedge would have gone up or stayed flat.
3 Add Real Hedges
If you're long risk-on assets, add risk-off hedges: gold, long USD, long volatility (VIX), or bonds. These should go up when equities go down. That's real diversification.

The Trading Journal

The most important risk tool you own. Every trade logged: entry, exit, R:R planned vs actual, emotional state, what you saw vs what happened. Pattern recognition over 50+ trades reveals YOUR specific risk tendencies.

You can read every risk management book in the world. But until you log 50 trades and see the pattern of where you blow up, you're guessing. Your journal shows you the truth.

What a Trade Journal Entry Should Include

1 Trade Setup
Date, ticker, entry price, stop loss, target, planned R:R ratio, position size, and why you took the trade (structure, trigger, alignment, confirmation — STACK).
2 Execution & Exit
Actual exit price, time in trade, actual R:R ratio (what you actually made/lost), exit reason (hit stop, hit target, discretionary exit, time stop, etc.).
3 Emotional State & Review
Emotional state during trade (confident, worried, FOMO, revenge mentality, etc.), did you follow your plan?, what did you see that the chart didn't show you?, one sentence on what you'd do differently next time.

After 50 trades, review. You'll see patterns: "I break my stops 40% of the time." "I overtrade after losses." "I hold winners too long." This is the data that fixes you.

Emotional Risk Management

The five killers: Revenge Trading, FOMO, Overtrading, Moving Stops, and Refusing to Cut Losses. Each one looks different. Each one has the same outcome: account destruction.

The Five Killers & How to Prevent Them

1 Revenge Trading
What it looks like: You take a loss. You immediately take another trade to "make it back." You're not thinking clearly. You're angry.
Why it happens: Loss aversion is real. Your brain wants to recover the loss immediately.
The rule that prevents it: You hit your daily max loss. You stop trading. Period. Revenge trading happens when you keep trading after you should have stopped.
2 FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)
What it looks like: Bitcoin just spiked 10%. Everyone is talking about it. You chase the move, ignoring your plan.
Why it happens: Social pressure and the fear of being left behind are powerful.
The rule that prevents it: Only trade setups on your watchlist. If it wasn't on your list before the spike, it's not a setup — it's a chase.
3 Overtrading
What it looks like: You take 10 trades today instead of your usual 2. You're bored, restless, or feel pressure to "make money."
Why it happens: Trading feels good (dopamine hit). More trades = more dopamine. You're not trading a setup, you're chasing a feeling.
The rule that prevents it: Daily trade limit. Only X trades per day. When you hit it, you're done.
4 Moving Stops
What it looks like: Your stop is at 95. Price drops to 96. You move your stop to 90. Price drops to 89. Now you're in a disaster.
Why it happens: You don't want to take the loss. You rationalize that "it might come back."
The rule that prevents it: Your stop is SET BEFORE YOU ENTER. Not after. Once you enter, the stop does not move up — only down (on winners).
5 Refusing to Cut Losses
What it looks like: You're down 10% on a position. You refuse to sell. "It'll come back." Price drops 30%. Now you're in a nightmare.
Why it happens: Admitting a loss feels like failure. Your ego fights the stop.
The rule that prevents it: Your stop is automatic. You hit it, you exit. No thinking. No negotiating. The stop exists so you don't have to make the emotional decision.

The K in STACK: Keep Discipline

This ties it all together. Risk management IS the K in STACK. Without it, Structure, Trigger, Alignment, and Confirmation don't matter.

You can have a perfect setup. You can see the structure, hit the trigger, maintain alignment, and get confirmation. But if you take a 10% loss on a position that should have been capped at 1%, you've broken the system. One bad loss can wipe out 10 good trades.

This is why K — Keep Discipline — is the last letter. It's the foundation. It's what makes the other four letters matter.

"You don't rush a roux. You don't rush a trade. And once you start building, you don't stop checking the temperature. Risk management is the thermometer." — CreoleTrades Doctrine

The Roux Score isn't complete without risk management. Period.

Key Takeaways

  • Losing 50% requires 100% gain to break even — this asymmetry is why preventing big losses matters more than chasing big wins
  • Set daily, weekly, and monthly drawdown limits — when you hit them, you stop trading, no exceptions
  • Real diversification means holdings that move opposite to each other — not all risk-on assets correlated to the same risk-off event
  • Your trading journal reveals your personal risk patterns over 50+ trades — this is your blueprint for fixing yourself
  • The five killers (revenge trading, FOMO, overtrading, moving stops, refusing losses) all share one solution: rules that execute automatically, without emotion
  • Risk management is the foundation of STACK — without the K (Keep Discipline), the other letters don't matter

Practice Exercise

Start a trading journal today. It can be a spreadsheet, a notebook, or an app. Log your next 10 trades with: ticker, entry, stop, target, actual exit, R:R planned vs actual, and a 1-sentence note on your emotional state. After 10 trades, review: where did you break your own rules?
