Chart Patterns

Why Patterns Work

Chart patterns work because thousands of traders see the same thing and act on it. They're self-fulfilling prophecies backed by crowd psychology.

When enough people see a double bottom and buy, the price goes up. When enough traders recognize a bearish head and shoulders and sell, the price falls. The pattern becomes the reality because the crowd believes in it.

But patterns fail too. And knowing when they fail is just as important as knowing the pattern itself. A double bottom can break down. A bull flag can reverse. The professionals distinguish themselves by knowing when to trust a pattern and when to exit when it breaks.

The key: patterns are probabilities, not certainties. They increase your edge, but they don't guarantee a win.

Reversal Patterns

Reversal patterns signal a change in trend. Uptrend ends. Downtrend ends. These patterns mark the turning point.

Double Top

Shape
M-shaped pattern. Price hits resistance at level A, pulls back, hits the same level again, and fails.
Signal
Bearish reversal. The market tested higher twice and twice rejected it. Sellers are in control.
Entry
Short sell when price breaks below the neckline (the low between the two tops).
Target
Measure the height from the neckline to the tops. That distance is your downside target.

Double Bottom

Shape
W-shaped pattern. Price hits support at level A, bounces up, tests the same level again, and holds.
Signal
Bullish reversal. The market tested lower twice and twice rejected it. Buyers are in control.
Entry
Buy when price breaks above the neckline (the high between the two bottoms).
Target
Measure the height from the neckline to the bottoms. That distance is your upside target.

Head & Shoulders

Shape
Three peaks: left shoulder, taller head in the middle, right shoulder at similar height to the left. Neckline connects the lows between them.
Signal
Bearish reversal. The pattern shows weakening momentum: left shoulder high, head higher (last attempt up), right shoulder lower (failure to sustain).
Entry
Short sell when price breaks below the neckline. This is your confirmation the pattern has triggered.
Stop Loss
Place above the right shoulder. If price exceeds that level, the pattern is invalidated.

Continuation Patterns

Continuation patterns show a temporary pause in the trend. The trend is not reversing; it's just taking a breath before resuming.

Bull Flag

How to Identify

1 The Pole
A strong, steep upward move. This is the flagpole. It establishes the strength of the trend.
2 The Flag
A small pullback that forms a channel or range (consolidation). The pullback is orderly, showing controlled selling. This is where most traders take profits or get shaken out.
3 The Breakout
Price breaks above the flag's resistance and continues upward. The trend resumes. Entry is when price breaks above the flag resistance. Stop loss is below the flag's support.

Bear Flag

How to Identify

1 The Pole
A strong, steep downward move. The flagpole establishes the trend's strength.
2 The Flag
A small bounce that forms a range (consolidation). The bounce is orderly, showing controlled buying. Sellers are waiting to re-enter at the flag resistance.
3 The Breakout
Price breaks below the flag's support and continues downward. Entry is when price breaks below flag support. Stop loss is above the flag's resistance.

Ascending Triangle

Shape
Flat top resistance (horizontal line) with rising lows (support line moves higher each time).
Signal
Usually breaks up. Buyers are getting stronger (higher lows) while resistance holds. When it breaks, it breaks hard.
Entry
Buy when price breaks above the flat top resistance.
Stop
Below the most recent swing low within the triangle.

Descending Triangle

Shape
Flat bottom support (horizontal line) with falling highs (resistance line moves lower each time).
Signal
Usually breaks down. Sellers are getting stronger (lower highs) while support holds. When it breaks, it breaks hard.
Entry
Short when price breaks below the flat bottom support.
Stop
Above the most recent swing high within the triangle.

Wedge Patterns

Wedges trick beginners because they look bullish or bearish, but often resolve opposite to what you'd expect.

Rising Wedge

Important: Despite looking bullish (going up), rising wedges typically break DOWN.

Shape
Higher highs AND higher lows, but the space between them is shrinking. Price is rising but with weakening momentum.
Signal
Bearish. The buying momentum is weakening. Each higher high gets harder to achieve. Sellers are stepping in earlier.
Entry
Short when price breaks below the rising support line of the wedge.
Why It Fails Upward
Volume and momentum are declining even though price is rising. This is unsustainable. When it exhausts, it reverses hard.

Falling Wedge

Important: Despite looking bearish (going down), falling wedges typically break UP.

Shape
Lower highs AND lower lows, but the space between them is shrinking. Price is falling but with weakening downside momentum.
Signal
Bullish. The selling momentum is weakening. Each lower low is less severe. Buyers are stepping in earlier.
Entry
Buy when price breaks above the falling resistance line of the wedge.
Why It Fails Downward
Volume and momentum are declining even though price is falling. Sellers are losing strength. When it exhausts, buyers step in and reverse it.

Pattern Failures & How to Handle Them

Every pattern can fail. A double bottom can break down instead of up. A bull flag can reverse. A head and shoulders can break above the neckline instead of below it.

This is not a flaw in the pattern. This is the market being unpredictable. The professionals don't fight the market; they exit their position.

The rule: Always set your stop loss based on the pattern invalidation level. If the pattern breaks, you're out. No arguing with the chart.

  • For a double bottom, if price breaks below the first bottom, the pattern failed. Exit the long position.
  • For a head and shoulders, if price goes above the right shoulder, the pattern failed. Exit the short position.
  • For a bull flag, if price breaks below the flag support, the pattern failed. Exit the long.
  • For a rising wedge, if price breaks above the wedge resistance, the pattern may have failed. Re-evaluate.

Pattern failures are part of trading. They happen because markets are driven by news, earnings, geopolitics, and thousands of other factors. Patterns are not magic; they're probabilities. Sometimes the probability doesn't play out.

The skill is recognizing the failure early and exiting with a small loss instead of holding for a big one.

Key Takeaways

  1. Chart patterns work because crowds of traders act on them. They're self-fulfilling prophecies with real edge.
  2. Reversal patterns (double top, double bottom, head & shoulders) mark trend changes. They show exhaustion of the current trend.
  3. Continuation patterns (bull flag, bear flag, triangles) show the trend pausing, not reversing. Expect the trend to resume after a breakout.
  4. Wedges are tricky: rising wedges usually break DOWN (despite looking bullish), and falling wedges usually break UP (despite looking bearish).
  5. Pattern failures happen. Every pattern can fail. Set your stop loss at the invalidation level and exit if the pattern breaks.
  6. Patterns are probabilities, not certainties. They increase your edge, but the market respects the stop loss more than the pattern.

Practice Exercise

Pick SPY or QQQ on the daily chart. Scroll through the last 6 months and identify at least 2 chart patterns. Screenshot them. For each pattern, note: (1) What pattern is it? (2) Where would the entry be? (3) Where would the stop loss go? (4) Did it work or fail? Compare your analysis to what actually happened on the chart.
