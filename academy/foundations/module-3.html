What Is an ETF?

An Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF) is an investment fund that trades on stock exchanges like individual stocks. Unlike mutual funds, which are priced once per day, ETFs can be bought and sold throughout the trading day at market prices.

The core idea: You don't need to pick individual stocks to trade the market. ETFs give you exposure to entire sectors, indices, or asset classes with one ticker symbol. Instead of buying 500 companies individually to track the S&P 500, you buy one share of SPY.

This is the foundation of modern trading. Most professional traders don't trade individual stocks—they trade ETFs because the volume is massive, the spreads are tight, and the liquidity is unmatched.

ETF Exchange-Traded Fund—a basket of assets traded like a stock
Index A group of assets assembled to measure market performance
Benchmark The standard used to measure the performance of an ETF
Expense Ratio Annual cost of holding the ETF (usually 0.03% to 0.5%)
Liquidity How easily you can buy/sell without moving the price
AUM Assets Under Management—total value of the fund

The Big Three: SPY, QQQ, IWM

If you trade ETFs, you need to know these three. They are the most liquid, most traded ETFs on the planet. SPY alone does billions in volume daily. If you're starting out, these are your arena.

SPY — The S&P 500

Tracks the 500 largest US companies. This is the broad market. When someone says "the market is up," they're usually talking about SPY or the S&P 500. SPY is diversified across sectors and company sizes. It's the safest of the three and the most liquid. Most beginners start here.

QQQ — The Nasdaq 100

Tracks 100 of the largest non-financial US companies listed on the Nasdaq. This is heavily weighted toward tech—think Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Tesla. QQQ moves faster than SPY and is more volatile. When tech is leading, QQQ leads. When tech sells off, QQQ drops hardest.

IWM — The Russell 2000

Tracks 2,000 smaller US companies. This is the small-cap index. IWM is more volatile than both SPY and QQQ and moves on different catalysts. When the market is in "risk-on" mode and investors feel confident, small caps lead. When fear kicks in, they sell off first.

Comparing the Big Three

SPY (S&P 500)

Broad market exposure, large-cap companies, most stable, highest volume. Best for: trading the general market direction.

QQQ (Nasdaq 100)

Tech-heavy, growth-focused, more volatile, fastest moving. Best for: capturing uptrends in growth and technology sectors.

IWM (Russell 2000)

Small-cap exposure, highest volatility, leads in risk-on markets. Best for: trading micro trends and high-conviction setups.

Sector ETFs

When you hear traders talk about "sector rotation," they're talking about money moving between these sector ETFs. Each one tracks a different industry:

XLE (Energy) – Oil, gas, and energy companies. When oil is surging (like right now), XLE runs hard.

XLF (Financials) – Banks and financial institutions. Trades on interest rates and credit spreads.

XLK (Technology) – Software, hardware, semiconductors. Leads in growth markets.

XLV (Healthcare) – Pharma, biotech, medical devices. Defensive play that holds up in downturns.

XLI (Industrials) – Manufacturing, construction, machinery. Trades on economic cycle and interest rates.

The key: Know where the money is flowing. If tech is in trouble but healthcare is rallying, sector rotation is happening. Smart traders follow the money.

Safe Haven & Commodity ETFs

These move on different catalysts than equities. They're your hedges and your alternative trades:

GLD (Gold)

When the stock market is in trouble, gold rises. It's a risk-off trade and an inflation hedge. Gold tends to rise when stocks fall.

TLT (20+ Year Treasuries)

A play on interest rates and risk sentiment. When the Fed is cutting rates or recession fears spike, TLT rallies. Like gold, it tends to rise when stocks fall.

USO (Crude Oil)

Energy and geopolitical play. Oil spikes on supply shocks, OPEC cuts, and geopolitical tension. Trades independently from stocks.

UNG (Natural Gas)

More volatile than crude oil. Trades on supply/demand, weather, and storage levels. Can make sharp moves in either direction.

These ETFs are critical for understanding market dynamics. Gold and TLT rising while stocks fall is a red flag. Oil spiking while stocks hold is a sign of inflation concerns.

Leveraged & Inverse ETFs

These are powerful but dangerous. Use them correctly and they're your secret weapon. Use them wrong and you'll blow up your account.

Leveraged ETFs

TQQQ – 3x leveraged QQQ. Moves three times as fast as QQQ. SPXL – 3x leveraged SPY.

Inverse ETFs

SQQQ – 3x inverse QQQ. Rises when QQQ falls. SPXS – 3x inverse SPY.

How Leverage Decay Works (The Math You Need to Know)

Step 1: Understand Daily Rebalancing

Leveraged ETFs rebalance every day to maintain their 3x multiplier. This creates "decay"—a slow loss of value over time, especially in choppy or sideways markets.

Step 2: The Risk

If QQQ goes up 10% then down 10%, QQQ is flat. But TQQQ (3x QQQ) is down 15% because of daily rebalancing. This is leverage decay in action.

Step 3: The Rule

NEVER hold leveraged or inverse ETFs overnight unless you understand this math and have a specific reason to. They're designed for single-day trades only.

Leveraged & Inverse ETFs: When to Use, When to Avoid

  • Use for: Day trading, capturing a specific intraday move, hedging a position for hours only
  • Avoid for: Overnight holds, weekly trades, long-term positions of any kind
  • If you're not 100% certain about leverage decay, don't touch these yet

Key Takeaways

  • ETFs are baskets of assets that trade like stocks—they're the foundation of modern trading
  • SPY, QQQ, and IWM are the big three—master these and you understand market structure
  • Sector ETFs show you where money is rotating—track them to find leading sectors
  • Gold (GLD) and Treasuries (TLT) are your hedges—they rise when stocks fall
  • Leveraged ETFs (TQQQ, SPXL) are 3x power—day-trade only, never hold overnight
  • Liquidity is everything—trade the most liquid ETFs on the planet for tight spreads and instant fills

Module Quiz

Question 1: What does SPY track?

Correct Answer: The S&P 500 index (500 of the largest US companies). SPY is the broad market—when someone says "the market," they usually mean SPY.

Question 2: Why should beginners avoid holding TQQQ overnight?

Correct Answer: Leveraged ETFs decay due to daily rebalancing—they're designed for single-day trades only, not long-term holds. If the market goes up 10% then down 10%, TQQQ will be down more than flat because of how leverage compounds daily.

Question 3: During a risk-off event, which ETFs typically rise?

Correct Answer: GLD (Gold) and TLT (Treasuries). In a risk-off event, investors sell stocks and buy safe-haven assets. Gold rises as an inflation hedge and safe store of value. Treasuries rise as interest rate expectations fall.

Question 4: QQQ is heavily weighted toward which sector?

Correct Answer: Technology. The Nasdaq 100 is loaded with tech companies—this is why QQQ moves faster than SPY in growth markets and sells off harder when tech is under pressure.

Question 5: What does "sector rotation" mean for ETF traders?

Correct Answer: Money shifts between sectors based on economic conditions—knowing which sector ETFs are leading tells you where institutional money is flowing. When energy starts rallying hard while tech stumbles, that's sector rotation happening in real-time.
