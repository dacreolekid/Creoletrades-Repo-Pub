Trendlines & Channels

1
The Simplest Tool in Trading

A trendline is just a straight line connecting two or more points. It's the most basic form of technical analysis — and one of the most powerful when drawn correctly.

"Two points make a line. Three points make it valid."

That's it. No indicators needed. No algorithms. Just price, wicks, and a line. Professional traders obsess over trendlines because they work. They show you exactly where buyers and sellers are committed, and they identify the exact moment that changes.

2
Drawing Trendlines

Uptrend line: Connect two or more higher lows. You're tracing the floor where buyers keep defending.

Downtrend line: Connect two or more lower highs. You're tracing the ceiling where sellers keep controlling.

Rules:

  • Use the WICK (shadow), not the body. The wick represents the full range of price action, including rejection points.
  • Need at least 2 touches to draw, 3+ to validate. Two points make a line, but it's not tested. Three touches mean you have confirmation.
  • The more touches, the stronger the trendline. A trendline that price has tested 5+ times is a significant level.

Three-step breakdown:

  1. How to draw on TradingView: Use the trendline tool (usually icon of a diagonal line), click two swing lows for uptrend or two swing highs for downtrend
  2. Wicks vs bodies: Always touch the wick (the thin shadow), not the thick body of the candle
  3. When to redraw: If price violates a trendline with a close below/above it, you may need to redraw using newer swing points
3
Trendline Breaks

When price breaks through a valid trendline, it signals a potential shift. But NOT every break is a reversal — sometimes it's a false break (wick pokes through, then reclaims).

Confirmation: Wait for a candle to CLOSE beyond the trendline. A wick poking through doesn't count. A full candle close below (on a downtrend) or above (on an uptrend) confirms the break.

Volume spike on the break adds confidence. If price closes beyond the trendline on high volume, the move is legitimate. If it's low volume, traders are not committed yet.

4
Ascending & Descending Channels

A channel is two parallel trendlines containing price.

Ascending channel: Higher highs + higher lows, price bounces between the lines. This is a bullish structure.

Descending channel: Lower highs + lower lows. This is a bearish structure.

Trading channels:

  • Buy at the support (lower line), sell at the resistance (upper line) — ride the channel
  • Breakout above = acceleration — trend is strengthening
  • Breakdown below = reversal — structure has broken

5
Horizontal vs Diagonal Levels

Trendlines are diagonal. They move with the trend.

Support/Resistance (next module) is horizontal. It stays flat.

The best setups happen when they CONVERGE — a horizontal support level meets a rising trendline. That's confluence, and it's where you want to be paying attention. When two or more levels overlap, that's where institutional money is sitting.

6
Key Takeaways
  • A trendline connects two or more swing points (highs or lows)
  • Two points make a line, three points validate it
  • Always use wicks, not candle bodies
  • A break is only confirmed when price closes beyond the trendline
  • Channels are two parallel trendlines that contain price action
  • Breakouts and breakdowns signal trend acceleration or reversal
  • Confluence happens when trendlines and support/resistance overlap — that's your highest probability setup

Practice Exercise

Open QQQ on the 1-hour chart. Draw one uptrend line and one downtrend line from the last 2 weeks.

  • Did price respect them?
  • Did any trendlines get broken?
  • When they broke, what happened next?

Write down your observations. Notice which trendline price respected most, and which one failed. This builds your intuition for trendline strength.

Module Quiz

1. How many touches does a trendline need to be considered valid? +

Answer: At least 3 touches. Two points create a line, but the third touch validates it. The more touches, the stronger the trendline.

2. When drawing an uptrend line, what do you connect? +

Answer: Higher lows — the swing lows that are progressively higher. You're tracing the floor where buyers keep defending price.

3. Should you use candle bodies or wicks when drawing trendlines? +

Answer: Wicks. They represent the full range of price action including rejection points. Candle bodies miss the true price extremes.

4. A price break through a trendline is confirmed when? +

Answer: A candle CLOSES beyond the trendline. A wick poking through doesn't count — price must fully close on the other side of the line to confirm the break.

5. What is a channel in technical analysis? +

Answer: Two parallel trendlines containing price — one connecting highs (resistance), one connecting lows (support) — creating a range for price to trade within.
