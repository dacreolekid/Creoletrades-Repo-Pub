Dollar Cost Averaging Glossary

All terms used in the DCA course, explained in plain English.

Dollar Cost Averaging (DCA)

A systematic investment strategy where you invest a fixed sum of money at regular intervals, regardless of the asset's current price. This removes timing risk and creates a disciplined approach to wealth-building.

Cost Basis

Your average cost per share or unit. With DCA, your cost basis naturally lowers over time as you buy more shares at lower prices. It's the foundation for calculating capital gains when you sell.

Compounding

When your investment returns generate their own returns. Your $100 earns $10, then that $10 earns $1—exponential growth. Over decades, compounding turns small contributions into massive wealth.

Value Averaging (VA)

A smarter cousin of DCA. Instead of fixed contributions, you invest whatever amount reaches your target portfolio value. You invest less in up markets, more in down markets—automating tactical buying.

Tactical DCA

Combining DCA with market awareness. You maintain baseline DCA but increase contributions during significant market dips (e.g., 15-20% drops). This lets you buy fear at discount prices systematically.

Cash-Secured Put

An options strategy where you sell a put option and collect premium income. If assigned, you buy shares at the strike price (usually a discount). If not assigned, you keep the premium and repeat.

Frequency

How often you invest: weekly, bi-weekly, monthly, quarterly. Monthly is standard. Shorter frequencies reduce timing risk but increase transaction costs. Longer frequencies are lazy but better than nothing.

Contribution Amount

How much you invest each period. Can be fixed ($500/month) or percentage-based (5-10% of income). Most professionals recommend 5-10% of gross income for aggressive wealth-building.

Rebalancing

Adjusting your portfolio back to your target allocation. Example: You wanted 60% stocks / 40% bonds, but it drifted to 70% / 30%. Rebalance by directing new contributions to underweight assets.

FIFO (First-In, First-Out)

A tax lot method where you sell your oldest shares first. Simple default but usually triggers higher capital gains because oldest shares often have bigger gains. Use only if you want simplicity over taxes.

Specific Lot Identification

Choosing which specific shares to sell when liquidating. You pick the highest-cost shares first, minimizing capital gains taxes. Requires record-keeping but can save thousands in taxes over time.

Capital Gains

Profit from selling an asset at a higher price than you bought it. Short-term gains (held <1 year) are taxed at ordinary income rates. Long-term gains (held >1 year) get preferential tax rates.

Index Funds / ETFs

Passive investments tracking a market index (S&P 500, total stock market). Low fees, broad diversification, perfect for DCA. Examples: SPY, VOO, VTI.

Brokerage

A platform where you buy and sell securities. Choose ones with low fees and automation (recurring investments). Examples: Fidelity, Charles Schwab, TD Ameritrade.

401(k) / IRA

Retirement accounts with tax advantages. 401(k) is employer-sponsored (often with matching). IRA is individual. Both support DCA automatically. Maximize these before regular brokerage accounts.

Volatility

Price swings. High volatility = wild price movements (crypto). Low volatility = steady price (bonds). DCA works best with high volatility because you buy more at lower prices.

Market Dip / Correction

A temporary price drop. Corrections are 10-20% drops. Bear markets are 20%+ drops. DCA investors celebrate dips because they buy more at discounts.

Lump Sum Investing

Investing all your money at once instead of over time. Historically wins 70% of the time BUT requires perfect timing. In real life, DCA wins because most people can't time the market.

Time Horizon

How long you plan to hold investments. Longer horizons (5+ years) let you weather volatility. DCA is designed for 10-20+ year horizons where compounding dominates.

Asset Allocation

How you distribute investments across asset classes (stocks, bonds, crypto, etc.). Example: 60% stocks / 30% bonds / 10% crypto. Your age and risk tolerance determine allocation.
