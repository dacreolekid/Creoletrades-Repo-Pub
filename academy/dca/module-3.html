Value Averaging: The Smarter Cousin of DCA

Value Averaging (VA) adjusts your contribution amount based on how your portfolio is performing. Instead of investing a fixed amount monthly, you invest whatever is needed to reach a target portfolio value at a specific date.

DCA vs Value Averaging

DCA: Invest $500 every month, period. Simple, automatic, no math.

VA: Invest whatever reaches your target. If your target is $5,000 after month 1 and the market has already gained you $500, you only invest $4,500. If it lost $500, you invest $5,500.

Month 1: Your Target is $5,000

Market is flat. You invest $5,000 to reach your target. Portfolio = $5,000.

Month 2: Your Target is $10,000

Market rallied and your $5,000 became $5,600. You only need $4,400 to reach $10,000. You invest less because the market helped you.

Month 3: Your Target is $15,000

Market crashed and your portfolio is now $8,000. You need $7,000 to reach $15,000. You invest more because the market hurt you.

The Power: Value Averaging automatically makes you buy more when the market is down and less when it's up—better than fixed DCA. It's the professional's DCA.

VA vs DCA

  • VA requires more active monitoring and math (most use spreadsheets).
  • VA typically outperforms DCA by 1-3% annually due to tactical buying.
  • Use VA if you're disciplined enough to calculate monthly targets.
  • Use DCA if you want set-and-forget simplicity.
  • Many professionals use hybrid: DCA baseline + VA adjustments on dips.

Tactical DCA: Combining DCA with Market Structure

Tactical DCA means increasing your contribution during market dips. You keep your baseline DCA, but when volatility spikes or prices drop 15-20%, you add extra capital.

How It Works

Baseline: $500/month regular DCA. Rule: When the market drops 15%, you add an extra $500 that month. When it drops 20%, you add $1,000. You're still disciplined (not panic-buying), but you're aggressive during fear.

Why This Works: Most investors do the opposite—they sell when markets crash and buy when they're high. Tactical DCA reverses this. You buy MORE when fear is highest. This is the essence of contrarian investing with a system.

The SMC Structure

If you know Smart Money Concepts (SMC) and supply/demand zones, tactical DCA becomes surgical. When price breaks through demand zones, you increase contributions. When it reclaims supply, you reduce. You're layering DCA into institutional structure.

Example: Bitcoin typically tests a demand zone, then rallies. DCA investors know this. As soon as it hits the demand zone (everyone's panicking), you deploy extra capital. When it recovers weeks later, you're already profitable.

DCA with Options: Cash-Secured Puts

Advanced move: Use cash-secured puts to DCA into positions at a discount. This is for experienced traders only.

The Strategy

Instead of buying 100 shares of a $50 stock for $5,000, you sell 1 put option at $45 strike, collecting a premium (say $200). You keep that premium. If assigned, you buy 100 shares at $45 instead of $50—a 10% discount. If not assigned, you keep the premium and collect again next month.

Why DCA + Puts Works: You get paid to wait. Your average cost basis gets crushed because you either buy at a discount or keep premium income. Over 2 years of selling monthly puts, you've reduced your cost basis by 15-20% compared to regular DCA.

Cash-Secured Put

You sell a put option, keeping the premium. If the stock falls below strike, you buy 100 shares at that strike price. If it doesn't, you keep the premium and repeat. You need cash reserved equal to the strike price.

Warning: This requires options approval and understanding of Greeks (delta, theta, vega). For most investors, simple DCA is better. Use this if you're experienced.

Building a Multi-Asset DCA Portfolio

Don't put all your DCA money in one asset. Build a balanced portfolio with target allocations:

Aggressive (Age 25-40)

70% stocks / 20% crypto / 10% bonds. High growth, accept volatility. DCA smooths the swings.

Moderate (Age 40-55)

50% stocks / 10% crypto / 30% bonds / 10% REITs. Balanced growth and stability.

Conservative (Age 55+)

30% stocks / 50% bonds / 15% REITs / 5% crypto. Capital preservation, modest growth.

How to Allocate DCA

If you're allocating 60% stocks / 30% bonds / 10% crypto and investing $1,000/month: $600 to stocks, $300 to bonds, $100 to crypto. Rebalance quarterly. This prevents concentration risk and keeps you disciplined across asset classes.

The Long Game: 5, 10, 20 Year Projections

Here's why DCA is so powerful. Let's project $500/month at different returns:

5 Years @ 8% Returns

$30,000 invested + ~$5,000 earnings = $35,000. You made $5,000 on nothing.

10 Years @ 10% Returns

$60,000 invested + ~$35,000 earnings = $95,000. Compounding is starting to roar.

20 Years @ 10% Returns

$120,000 invested + ~$380,000 earnings = $500,000. Your contributions are now <10% of your wealth. Compounding did the heavy lifting.

Key Insight: Year 1-5 is about discipline. Year 5-10 is about consistency. Year 10-20 is about compounding. Most people quit in year 3. Don't be most people.

Why Time is Everything

  • Start at 25 with $500/month? Age 65, you have $1M+ (10% returns).
  • Start at 35 with $500/month? Age 65, you have $500K. You missed 10 years of compounding.
  • Start at 45 with $500/month? Age 65, you have $150K. You gave up exponential growth.
  • The gap between starting early and late is often $500K+. That's not a small difference.

Key Takeaways

  • Value Averaging outperforms DCA by automatically adjusting contributions to market conditions.
  • Tactical DCA increases contributions during dips—buying fear when everyone else sells.
  • Cash-secured puts let you DCA at discounts (advanced strategy for experienced traders).
  • Multi-asset portfolios reduce concentration risk and keep you balanced across cycles.
  • 20-year projections show compounding turns small contributions into generational wealth.
  • The difference between starting at 25 vs 35 is often $500K+. Time is literally money.
  • DCA works because it removes emotion, increases contributions during dips, and compounds relentlessly.

Practice Exercise

Build a 20-year projection for your DCA plan. Use a spreadsheet: monthly contribution, assumed annual return (8-10%), target ending portfolio value. Now calculate: what would you need to earn annually to reach that target without DCA (lump sum)? Compare the numbers. This is the power of compounding made visible.
