You understand DCA. Now it's time to build YOUR plan. This module walks you through the exact decisions: what to buy, how often to buy it, how much to spend, where to buy it, and how to optimize taxes. This is where theory becomes your actual portfolio.

Choosing Your DCA Assets

The asset doesn't matter as much as your discipline to stick with it. That said, here are your main options:

Index Funds / ETFs

Low fees, broad diversification, passive. Perfect for DCA. Example: SPY, VOO, VTI track the entire market.

Individual Stocks

Higher volatility, requires research, higher potential returns. DCA helps smooth the volatility. Example: Tech, healthcare, dividend stocks.

Crypto

Extreme volatility, decentralized, 24/7 trading. DCA is ESSENTIAL here because timing is impossible. Bitcoin, Ethereum.

Bonds / Fixed Income

Lower volatility, steady returns, portfolio stability. DCA works but is less exciting. Mix with stocks for balance.

Pro Tip: Most wealth is built on boring assets. A portfolio of 80% index funds + 20% individual stock picks is better than 100% crypto or 100% penny stocks. DCA magnifies discipline, not risk.

Setting Your Contribution Frequency

How often should you invest? Weekly, bi-weekly, monthly, quarterly?

Weekly DCA

Best for high-volatility assets (crypto, speculative stocks). More frequent buying = more snapshots of price, smoother average. Requires automated setup.

Monthly DCA

The sweet spot for most investors. Aligns with payroll. Simple to remember. Reduces transaction fees. Used by 90% of serious DCA investors.

Quarterly or Annual

Lazy DCA. Better than lump sum, worse than monthly. Use this only if you're waiting for a bonus or tax refund.

Rule: Pick a frequency you can sustain forever. Skipping months defeats the purpose. Most professionals use monthly because it's automatic with payroll.

Determining Your Contribution Amount

There are two approaches: percentage of income or fixed amount.

Percentage-Based (Recommended)

Invest 5-10% of your gross income every month. If you earn $5,000/month, invest $250-500. As your income grows, your contribution grows automatically. This scales with your life.

Fixed Amount

Pick a number: $100, $500, $1,000 per month—and stick with it. Simple. But doesn't scale with income growth. Use this if your income is stable and predictable.

The Math: A 25-year-old investing $500/month at 8% annual returns will have $1.2M by age 65. A 35-year-old starting the same? $520K. Time is exponential. Start now, even if it's $50/month.

How Much Should You DCA?

  • Percentage method: 5-10% of gross income is the gold standard.
  • Fixed method: Pick an amount you won't skip, even in bad months.
  • Minimum: $50-100/month makes a difference over 20+ years.
  • Maximum: Only invest what you won't need for 5+ years.

Choosing Your Platform & Automation

DCA only works if it's automated. Set it and forget it. Here's where to DCA:

Brokerage Apps

TD Ameritrade, Fidelity, Charles Schwab. Offer automatic recurring investments, low fees. Best for stocks/ETFs.

Crypto Exchanges

Coinbase, Kraken, Binance have built-in DCA features. Set it up once, forget about it. Perfect for Bitcoin/Ethereum.

Robo-Advisors

Betterment, Wealthfront manage DCA for you automatically. Hands-off approach, slight fees, diversification included.

401(k) / IRA

Your retirement account is DCA on steroids—automatic payroll deduction, tax benefits, employer match. The most powerful tool available.

Best Practice: Use your employer's 401(k) first (especially if there's a match—that's free money). Then automate additional investment through a brokerage. DCA should require zero willpower.

Rebalancing: When and How

Rebalancing means adjusting your portfolio back to your target allocation. Example: You wanted 60% stocks / 40% bonds, but market gains made it 70% / 30%. Rebalance back to 60/40.

When to Rebalance

Annually or semi-annually. Check your portfolio once or twice a year. If allocations have drifted more than 5%, rebalance. Don't touch it monthly—that's noise.

How to Rebalance

Simple: Direct your next DCA contribution to the underweight asset. If stocks dropped to 55%, put this month's $500 entirely into stocks until you hit 60%. Let DCA do the rebalancing for you. Don't trigger unnecessary tax events.

Tax Consideration: Selling winners triggers capital gains tax. Instead, rebalance by directing new contributions to underweight positions. This is tax-efficient DCA.

Tax Optimization: FIFO & Specific Lot ID

When you sell DCA holdings, you need to account for the cost basis. There are two methods:

FIFO (First-In, First-Out)

You sell your oldest shares first. Simple, default method. Usually triggers higher capital gains because your oldest shares have the biggest gains. Use this if you want simplicity.

Specific Lot Identification (Recommended)

You choose WHICH shares to sell. Sell your highest-cost shares first to minimize gains. Requires record-keeping but saves taxes. This is the pro move.

Example

You bought 100 shares at $10, then 100 at $20. If forced to sell 50, FIFO sells the $10 shares (gains = $500). Specific ID sells 50 of the $20 shares (gains = $0). Specific ID saved you thousands in taxes.

Action Item: Check your brokerage settings. Ensure specific lot identification is your default. Your CPA will thank you.

Key Takeaways

  • Asset choice matters less than consistency. Pick boring (index funds) or exciting (crypto), but pick one.
  • Monthly DCA is the sweet spot—aligns with paychecks, requires no heroic discipline.
  • Invest 5-10% of income or a fixed amount you'll never skip. More matters less than consistency.
  • Automate everything. DCA only works if you forget about it.
  • Rebalance annually by directing new contributions to underweight positions.
  • Use specific lot identification for taxes—it can save you thousands.
  • Your 401(k) is the foundation; everything else is gravy.
